# Prologue: Tuesday Morning

---

Elena Vasquez was not the kind of person who changed the world. She knew this about herself the way she knew her shoe size — not with disappointment, not with pride, just with the settled clarity of a fact observed and accepted. She was good at her job. She was not extraordinary at her job. The distinction had stopped bothering her sometime around her thirty-eighth birthday, when she'd received tenure and realized that the anxiety she'd been carrying for a decade had quietly packed its bags and left, replaced by something more durable and less interesting: contentment.

She was forty-four. She had two PhDs — one in theoretical physics from MIT, the other in applied mathematics from Chicago, earned during a postdoctoral fellowship that had lasted two years longer than planned because she'd gotten interested in topological invariants and lost track of time. She had a faculty position at a university she liked, in a department she respected, in a town that was neither too large nor too small and that had a Thai restaurant on Elm Street that knew her order.

Pad see ew. Medium spice. Extra lime.

Her morning routine was a thing of mechanical beauty. Alarm at 6:15. Coffee by 6:20 — she prepped the machine the night before, a habit she'd acquired from her father, who had prepped his lunch the night before every working day for thirty years. Shower. Dress. The clothes were not adventurous — she owned eleven variations on the same outfit, a strategy she'd adopted after reading that decision fatigue was real and that she'd rather spend her finite cognitive resources on Lagrangians than on whether the blue blouse went with the grey slacks. It did. It always did. That was the point.

She drove to campus in a nine-year-old Honda Civic that had 142,000 miles on it and a check-engine light that had been on since March. She parked in the same spot — third row, fourth from the left, under the oak tree that dropped acorns on her windshield every October with a persistence she found privately admirable. She walked to her office. She unlocked the door. She set her bag on the chair, her coffee on the desk, and her phone face-down on the shelf, because the phone was a trap and she knew it.

This was Tuesday.

---

The department occupied the third floor of the Halverson Building, a concrete structure from the 1970s that had been designed, as far as anyone could tell, by someone who had strong feelings about right angles and no feelings whatsoever about natural light. The hallways were narrow. The offices were small. The radiators made a sound in winter that Elena had once described to a graduate student as "a cat being slowly translated into steam," and the student had laughed so hard he'd spilled his coffee, and Elena had liked that — the laugh, not the coffee — because it meant she was still funny, which was a thing she valued more than she let on.

She taught two courses: an undergraduate survey called Introduction to Quantum Mechanics, which she had taught eleven times and could now deliver in a moderate coma, and a graduate seminar on topological methods in field theory, which she loved and which had an enrollment of twelve, exactly the right number for the kind of conversation she wanted to have. Twelve students, each one smart enough to follow and uncertain enough to ask questions. She had no patience for students who pretended to understand. She had infinite patience for students who admitted they didn't.

Her colleagues were, by and large, decent people doing decent work. She liked most of them. She was close to a few.

David Okafor she had known since a conference in 2011 — a combinatorial number theorist at Stanford with a laugh that could be heard through walls and a mind that operated at a speed Elena found both inspiring and slightly exhausting. They spoke on the phone every few weeks, usually about mathematics, occasionally about David's ongoing campaign to convince his department to install a espresso machine that wasn't, in his words, "an insult to the concept of pressure." She respected David more than almost anyone in her field. He was the person she called when she encountered something she couldn't classify, because David's gift was not solving problems but *recognizing* them — seeing, in a tangle of data, the shape of the question that mattered.

Marcus Chen was newer — a computer scientist, hired three years ago, young, energetic, the kind of colleague who forwarded interesting links and organized department happy hours and remembered everyone's birthday. Elena liked Marcus in the uncomplicated way she liked people who were kind without agenda. He was not a close friend. He was a good neighbor. The distinction mattered to her — she had learned, over the years, that the colleagues who sustained you were not always the ones you ate lunch with. Sometimes they were the ones who sent you an email on a Tuesday that you almost didn't open.

But that hadn't happened yet.

---

Her research was solid. Not spectacular. She had published forty-one papers over eighteen years, a rate that her department chair described as "admirably consistent" during her last review, a phrase she had accepted with the same equanimity she accepted everything — as data, neither flattering nor damning, just true. Her h-index was 28, which placed her comfortably in the upper-middle tier of her field: cited enough to matter, not cited enough to be famous. Her most-referenced paper — on topological constraints in 2+1 dimensional field theories — had 340 citations, which she was privately proud of and publicly indifferent to, because pride was a luxury and indifference was a posture and the truth was somewhere between them, in the place where a person could look at their own career and say: *I did this. It was real work. It mattered to the twelve people who read it carefully.*

She was not competitive. This was unusual in academia and occasionally mistaken for lack of ambition. It was not lack of ambition. It was the presence of something she had never named — a quiet conviction that the work was the point, not the ranking, and that a paper that said something true was worth more than a paper that said something impressive. She had turned down two offers from higher-ranked departments because she liked her office and her students and the Thai restaurant on Elm Street, and because moving would have disrupted a rhythm she had spent fifteen years building, and rhythm, she believed, was how good work got done.

She had no children. She had been married once, briefly, in her early thirties, to a chemical engineer named Paul who was perfectly nice and profoundly wrong for her in ways that took three years of living together to articulate. They had divorced amicably. She sent him a birthday card every year. He sent one back. They did not otherwise communicate, and this arrangement suited them both, which was perhaps the most honest thing their marriage had produced.

She had friends. She had a sister in San Diego — Lucia, younger by four years, a middle school art teacher with two kids and an inexhaustible capacity for phone calls that Elena answered roughly half of, a ratio that Lucia accepted with the cheerful resignation of someone who had long ago stopped expecting her sister to be a different person. They were close in the way that siblings who lived far apart were close — connected by something deeper than frequency, sustained by the knowledge that the other person existed and was fine.

She was fine. She was more than fine. She was a forty-four-year-old tenured physicist with a routine and a research program and a Honda Civic and a Thai restaurant and a mug that said WORLD'S OKAYEST PHYSICIST, and she went to bed at 10:30 and woke up at 6:15 and drank her coffee and drove to campus and taught her classes and wrote her papers and lived her life with the steady, unspectacular competence of a person who had found her frequency and was content to resonate at it.

This was Tuesday.

It was the last ordinary Tuesday she would ever have.
