## THE EVIDENCE

Over eight hundred trepanned skulls have been recovered from pre-Columbian Peru — more than the rest of the world combined. The procedures span two thousand years, from approximately 400 BCE to 1500 CE. Survival rates, determined by the presence or absence of bone healing at the surgical margins, improved from roughly forty percent in the earliest period to ninety-one percent in the period from 1000 to 1400 CE. By the Inca period (1400-1500 CE), survival rates stabilized between seventy-five and eighty-three percent.

Some skulls were found with gold cranioplasties — gold plates covering the trepanation openings, in permanent contact with the brain's surface tissue.

In the Maya region, eighty-eight percent of examined skulls show intentional cranial deformation applied from infancy using compression boards. Supra-inial lesions — deliberate modifications to the back of the skull, in the region overlying the visual cortex — were documented as having been applied during childhood. One skull showed five healed trepanation openings, each at a different location, each healed sequentially.

The procedures were performed on both sexes. The tools included obsidian blades (fracturing to a monomolecular edge, sharper than any modern steel scalpel) and, in later periods, copper and bronze tumi knives. Coca leaves and herbal preparations provided analgesia and antisepsis. The procedures were performed in open-air ceremonial environments.
