### C. The Gap-Fill Principle

**"There are always enough in the NEXT tier to simply fill EACH GAP made by the prior circular array."**

This is the geometric key:

**A 2-strand helix** arranges two strands at 180° apart in the cross-section (0° and 180°). This leaves TWO gaps — at 90° and 270°.

**A 3-strand helix** arranges three strands at 120° apart (0°, 120°, 240°). The third strand occupies a position that FILLS one of the binary gaps. But it creates NEW gaps.

**A 5-strand helix** arranges five strands at 72° apart (0°, 72°, 144°, 216°, 288°). The two new strands fill the gaps left by the ternary arrangement. New gaps are created.

**A 7-strand helix** arranges seven strands at ~51.4° apart. The two new strands fill the gaps left by the quinary arrangement.

**The pattern:** Each successive prime helix count provides EXACTLY enough new strands to fill the gaps of the prior arrangement, while creating new (smaller) gaps for the next tier to fill. The filling is FRACTAL — each tier's gaps are smaller than the prior tier's, requiring finer strands, which the next prime provides.

The angular gaps decrease as: 180° → 120° → 72° → 51.4° → 32.7° → 27.7° → ...

Each tier's strands fit into the angular space left by the prior tier. The successive primes ARE the gap-fillers. The geometry REQUIRES primes because only primes produce angular positions that don't overlap with any prior tier's positions.

### D. Why Primes (Geometrically)

If you added a 4th strand (composite: 2×2), it would land at 90° — a position that COINCIDES with an existing binary gap, yes, but also creates a SYMMETRY with the binary pair that makes the structure degenerate (it becomes two superimposed double-helices rather than one quad-helix). Composites produce degenerate symmetries. PRIMES produce non-degenerate arrangements — each strand occupies a unique angular position that doesn't reduce to a sub-pattern.

The helix expansion MUST proceed through primes to avoid degeneracy. 2 → 3 → 5 → 7 → 11 → 13 → ... This is the same divergence tree as the chip's base expansion, but implemented as spiral geometry rather than lattice geometry.
