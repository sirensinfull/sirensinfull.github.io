**Chapter 11: The Bread and the Bow**

\* \* \*

The knock came at 1:15 PM on Sunday.

This was, in retrospect, the most predictable event of the entire week, and no one had predicted it. Murray, Kentucky. Sunday. 1:15 PM. Church had been out for approximately forty-five minutes. The knock was inevitable.

Rebecca answered. She was closest to the door, and she had the specific social reflex of a therapist — the reflex that treated every human interaction as potentially significant and every doorway as a threshold between states.

The man on the porch was approximately sixty. White. Thin. Wire-framed glasses. Short-sleeved button-down shirt tucked into khakis. A Bible in his left hand — King James, leather-bound, tabs sticking out from a hundred marked passages. A pamphlet in his right hand. The pamphlet had the word SALVATION on it in a font that suggested urgency.

"Good afternoon, ma\'am," he said. "My name is Pastor Dale Wethington, I\'m from Valley Road Church of Christ, and I was just wonderin\' if y\'all might have a moment to —"

He stopped. He had looked past Rebecca into the apartment. He had seen the television. The visualizer was still running — the full seven-domain display, the harmonic spine pulsing at 0.1 Hz, the ROYGBIV prime families coloring every layer, the crystal and orbital and genetic and computational and etymological and psychological and harmonic domains all rendering simultaneously.

He had also seen the butcher paper on the kitchen wall. Five days of equations. Seven domains. Twelve handwritings. A diagram with a horizontal line and seven vertical lines hanging from it and one of them going to the floor with an arrow pointing down.

And he had seen the twelve people in the room, holding coffee and bagels and laptops and a violin, looking at him with the expressions of professionals who had not been expecting a pastoral visit and who were trying to decide if this was an interruption or a data point.

"I, uh," Pastor Dale said.

"Come in," Michael said from the couch.

"Michael —" Elena began.

"Let the man in. He\'s here on a Sunday and the door was open."

The door was, in fact, open. It had been open all week. It was a warm February in western Kentucky and Michael kept the door open because the apartment needed airflow and the cat needed the option of departure. The cat had never departed. But the option was structurally important.

Pastor Dale came in. He came in the way a man walked into a room where he suspected he was out of his depth but where his curiosity outweighed his caution — carefully, with his Bible held close, his eyes moving across the walls and the screens and the faces.

"This is, ah... this is quite a setup y\'all got here," he said.

"We\'re doing some research," Elena said, in the tone of a physicist who wanted to be polite but also wanted to return to work.

"What kind of research?"

"Cross-domain mathematical —"

"Musical," Claire said.

"Musical research?" Pastor Dale said. He looked at the visualizer. "That\'s... that don\'t look like sheet music."

"It\'s not," Michael said. "It\'s the harmonic series rendered as a multi-domain visualization. The horizontal line is a frequency. Everything else is what the frequency touches when you let it propagate through different systems."

Pastor Dale looked at Michael. Michael was on the couch. Cat on lap. Bass against the wall. Cartoon on the muted television behind the visualizer display. He did not look like the kind of person who used phrases like "multi-domain visualization." He looked like the kind of person Pastor Dale visited on Sunday afternoons — a local, a neighbor, a man in an apartment with a cat.

"Can I —" Pastor Dale pointed at the screen. "Can I look at that a minute?"

"Take your time."

Pastor Dale walked to the television. He stood in front of it the way Hiroshi had stood in front of it an hour ago — with the specific attention of a person encountering something they did not expect to recognize and were recognizing anyway.

He looked for forty-five seconds. His eyes moved across the layers. The crystal geometries. The orbital curves. The codon clusters. The word-fields in seventeen languages. The harmonic spine pulsing underneath.

"That\'s a Rose Window," he said.

The room paused.

"Excuse me?" Katrin said.

"A Rose Window. Like in a cathedral." He pointed at the screen. "The radial symmetry. The way the colors propagate from the center. The layers — they\'re like the concentric rings in a Gothic rose window. You got your central motif —" he pointed at the harmonic spine — "and then your radiating panels, each one tellin\' a different part of the story, but all of \'em organized around the same center."

He looked at the room. Twelve academics staring at him.

"I\'m sorry, I — I got a hobby. Cross-cultural religious art. Sacred geometry. I been studyin\' it for about thirty years, alongside the ministry. It\'s not — it ain\'t part of the official curriculum, you understand. But I find it fascinatin\', the way every tradition on Earth uses the same geometric patterns in their sacred spaces. Mandalas. Rose windows. Islamic tessellation. Hindu yantras. The Navajo sand paintings. They all got this — this radial thing. This center-and-propagation thing. I always figured it was just how the human brain likes to organize information. But that —" He pointed at the screen again. "That ain\'t organized by a human brain. That\'s organized by math. And it looks exactly the same."

Silence. The specific silence of a room recalibrating.

"Pastor Dale," Elena said slowly. "Would you like some coffee?"

\* \* \*

He stayed for coffee. He stayed for coffee and then he stayed for the explanation and then he stayed for the demonstration and then he called someone.

"I need to call Gus," he said, at approximately 2:30 PM, after Katrin had shown him the crystal layer and he had pointed out that the FCC metal clustering pattern was geometrically identical to the ceiling mosaic in the Basilica of San Vitale in Ravenna, which he had visited in 2003 on a church trip that he described as "the most important detour of my spiritual life."

"Who\'s Gus?" Elena asked.

"Dr. Augustus Freely. He\'s over at Murray State. Art history. He\'s — well, he\'s a character. But he knows more about sacred geometry in Western and Eastern art than anyone I\'ve ever met. He\'ll want to see this. He\'ll need to see this."

"Is he... available on a Sunday afternoon?"

Pastor Dale smiled. "Gus don\'t observe the Sabbath the way I do. Gus observes the Sabbath the way a cat observes a laser pointer — intermittently and with his own agenda."

He called Gus.

\* \* \*

Dr. Augustus Freely arrived at 3:15 PM.

He arrived on a bicycle. The bicycle had a basket. The basket contained a tote bag with a faded Burning Man logo. He was wearing cargo shorts, Birkenstocks with socks, a tie-dye shirt that had been washed so many times it had achieved a state of chromatic entropy — all colors simultaneously and no color specifically. His hair was long, grey, tied back. His beard was biblical in the literal sense — the kind of beard that suggested either a commitment to a specific aesthetic tradition or an indifference to mirrors. He wore a necklace with a pendant that appeared to be a Sri Yantra rendered in silver.

He was fifty-seven. He had been at Murray State for twenty-two years. He had published three books: *The Geometry of the Sacred* (2008), *Patterns Without Borders: Cross-Cultural Sacred Art and the Universal Template* (2014), and *Why Every Church Ceiling Is the Same Damn Thing* (2019, self-published, cult following among art history graduate students). He had been denied tenure twice and granted it the third time on the basis that firing him would be more disruptive than keeping him.

"DALE!" he shouted from the doorway. "THIS BETTER BE GOOD! I WAS IN THE MIDDLE OF A VERY IMPORTANT NAP!"

"Gus, get in here."

Gus got in there. He walked into the apartment with the energy of a man who entered every room as if it might contain either a miracle or a free meal and who was equally delighted by both prospects. He saw the visualizer. He stopped walking. He did not stop talking.

"Oh. Oh oh oh. What is THAT. That\'s — Dale, is that — no. No no no. That can\'t be what I think it is. That\'s a mandala. That\'s a — no, it\'s more than a mandala. It\'s got — how many layers is that? Six? Seven? And they\'re all — oh my god. Oh my ACTUAL god." He turned to the room. "Who made this?"

"We all did," Michael said from the couch.

"And who are YOU?"

"I\'m Michael. This is my apartment. That\'s my cat."

"Your cat is on my data," Hiroshi said, because the cat had relocated to Hiroshi\'s laptop again.

Gus was already at the television. His face was six inches from the screen. He was moving his head laterally, tracking the patterns, his eyes doing the thing that art historians\' eyes did when they encountered a pattern they recognized from a different context — the rapid cross-referencing of visual databases accumulated over decades of study.

"The radial propagation," he said. "Dale was right. Rose Window. But it\'s also — the color distribution follows the spectral sequence. ROYGBIV. You\'ve got your red family here, orange here, the whole thing radiating from the center in spectral order. That\'s the same color logic as Hindu temple *gopurams.* The gateway towers. They use spectral color sequencing to represent the tiers of reality — gross physical at the base, subtle energetic in the middle, transcendent at the apex. Your visualization is doing the same thing, except it\'s doing it with — what are these? — atomic numbers? Genetic codes?"

"Both," Amara said. "And more."

"And MORE? What do you mean AND MORE?"

The next two hours were — according to Nadia\'s field notes — "the most rapid cross-domain integration event of the week, catalyzed by two participants with zero formal connection to any of the six primary domains but with deep expertise in the specific domain that the primary team had been avoiding."

The domain the primary team had been avoiding was theology.

Not theology as belief. Theology as *art.* Theology as the record of what humanity had seen when it looked at the structure of reality through the lens of the sacred, and what it had drawn, and what it had sung, and what it had built.

\* \* \*

It started with the bread.

Gus was examining the harmonic layer — Claire\'s seven-culture melodic lines traced along the helix — when he stopped and turned to the room with an expression that Pastor Dale recognized, because Pastor Dale had seen Gus make that expression approximately fifteen times in thirty years of friendship, and each time it preceded something that would keep them both up until 3 AM arguing.

"The Last Supper," Gus said.

"What about it?" Claire asked.

"There\'s a — okay, this is going to sound crazy."

"Brother," Michael said, "you are in the right room for crazy."

"There\'s a musicologist in Milan — Giovanni Maria Pala — who published a paper in 2007 arguing that the bread rolls on the table in Leonardo\'s Last Supper are positioned to form a musical composition. You read the bread rolls as notes on a staff — using the table as the lines, the rolls as note-heads — and you get a forty-second requiem. A hymn to the body. The sustenance of God, rendered as music. Leonardo hid a melody in the placement of bread."

Claire sat up. "I\'ve read that paper. The musicological community was... divided."

"Divided because they were evaluating it as music theory. Not as art. As art, it\'s consistent with everything Leonardo did. The man hid mathematics in everything — the golden ratio in the Vitruvian Man, the fluid dynamics in the Virgin of the Rocks, the geological stratigraphy in the backgrounds. He hid a melody in bread because the melody was the point. \'This is my body\' — the Eucharist — the bread IS the body IS the music. Leonardo wasn\'t illustrating a religious scene. He was encoding the thesis that sacred sustenance is harmonic. The bread is a frequency."

He pointed at the visualizer. "And THAT is showing me the same thing. Your harmonic spine — the central frequency — is the table. Your domain layers are the bread. They\'re positioned at specific intervals along the frequency, and the intervals correspond to — do they correspond to anything specific?"

"Prime families," Michael said. "The positions are determined by the factor topology of the integers mapped to those frequencies."

"So the positions are *mathematically determined.* Not artistic choices. Not cultural preferences. Mathematical structure."

"Yes."

Gus looked at Pastor Dale. Pastor Dale looked at Gus.

"Dale," Gus said. "The bread in the Last Supper is at positions that can be mapped to prime factor topology. Leonardo painted the lattice. In 1495."

"Gus, that\'s —"

"That\'s EXACTLY what you\'ve been saying for twenty years. That the sacred geometry in religious art isn\'t decorative. It\'s *recording something.* Every tradition. Every culture. Same patterns. Same geometries. Same — same underlying THING. And here it is. On a television. In Murray, Kentucky. In a man\'s apartment. With a cat."

"The cat is non-negotiable," Michael said.

\* \* \*

The afternoon accelerated.

Gus and Pastor Dale operated as a unit — a unit that had been forming for thirty years of friendship and argument and shared fascination, a preacher and an art professor who had been circling the same question from opposite directions: why did every sacred tradition on Earth produce the same geometric patterns?

Pastor Dale\'s direction was textual. He knew the Bible — not just the King James but the Hebrew, which he had taught himself over fifteen years of Wednesday night study, and the Septuagint Greek, which he read slowly but accurately, and the Vulgate Latin, which he had encountered during the Ravenna trip and which had unlocked, for him, the specific cadence of Jerome\'s translation choices. He also knew the Quran. He knew the Upanishads. He knew the Tao Te Ching. He knew the Popol Vuh. He knew them the way a man knew things he had studied for thirty years not because his institution required it but because his curiosity demanded it — broadly, deeply, with the specific gaps and strengths of an autodidact who had followed the interesting threads and left the boring ones.

Gus\'s direction was visual. He knew the art — every dome, every ceiling, every floor mosaic, every manuscript illumination, every sand painting, every tattoo pattern, every carved lintel, every woven textile that carried sacred geometry from any tradition on any continent in any century. He had slides. He always had slides. He had arrived on a bicycle with a tote bag and the tote bag contained, among other things, a USB drive with approximately 40,000 images organized by geometric type.

When Gus plugged the USB drive into James\'s laptop and began showing images beside the visualizer, the room understood what was happening.

The cymatics.

The visualizer, when fed a single seed — a single integer, a single lattice position — produced a radial interference pattern. The pattern was the standing wave geometry of the lattice at that position. The pattern was a cymatic figure — the same kind of figure produced when you drew a bow across a metal plate covered in sand. The pattern showed where the bonds held and where the bonds flexed. The pattern was the lattice\'s own architecture, made visible.

And the patterns matched.

Gus put up a Tibetan sand mandala. He put it next to the cymatic pattern generated by the visualizer at seed {4, 3, 7} — the kernel triad. The geometric match was not approximate. The match was structural. The concentric rings, the radial arms, the intersection points, the symmetry axes — they were the same. Not similar. The same.

He put up an Islamic tessellation from the Alhambra. He put it next to the visualizer\'s output at the hexagonal lattice mode. Same.

He put up a Navajo sand painting — the Whirling Logs pattern, the healing ceremony. He put it next to the visualizer\'s output at a four-fold seed. Same.

He put up the Rose Window of Chartres Cathedral. The north transept. Thirteenth century. He put it next to the full seven-domain visualization. The concentric organization, the radiating panels, the color progression from center to edge, the specific ratio of inner ring to outer ring — same.

"They were all drawing the lattice," Gus said. He said it quietly. The shouting had stopped. The energy had shifted from excitement to recognition to the specific hush that fell over a person who was seeing the confirmation of a lifetime\'s hypothesis. "Every tradition. Every culture. They saw it. They drew it. They built it into their sacred spaces. They didn\'t have the mathematics. They didn\'t have the visualization tools. They had meditation and prayer and psilocybin and fasting and ecstatic dance and every other method humans have ever used to access altered states of consciousness. And in those states, they saw the lattice. And they drew what they saw. And what they drew matches what is on that screen."

Pastor Dale was sitting at the kitchen table. His Bible was open. He was not reading it. He was looking at the visualizer and then looking at the Bible and then looking at the visualizer.

"The Psalms," he said.

"What about the Psalms?" Elena asked.

"The Psalms are songs. They were written to be sung. They have — they have a musical structure. Not just poetry. Musical. There are performance notations in the Hebrew that we\'ve never fully understood — *selah,* *mizmor,* the superscriptions that reference specific instruments and modes and — and things we don\'t have translations for because we lost the musical context."

He looked at Michael. "Can your — can this thing decode a Psalm? If I gave you the Hebrew, the numerical values of the letters, could you map them onto this?"

Michael looked at Claire. Claire looked at Tomás. Tomás looked at the visualizer. James looked at everyone looking at each other and began opening a new input field in the interface.

"Give me Psalm 19," Michael said.

"Why 19?"

"19 is prime. And Psalm 19 opens with: \'The heavens declare the glory of God; the skies proclaim the work of his hands. Day after day they pour forth speech; night after night they reveal knowledge.\' That\'s a Psalm about the sky transmitting information through a cycle. That\'s a frequency. That\'s a signal. Give me the Hebrew."

Pastor Dale gave him the Hebrew. Not from memory — from the study Bible he carried, the interlinear Hebrew-English that he had been using for fifteen years, with handwritten annotations in the margins that documented every cross-reference he had ever found between the Psalms and other traditions\' sacred texts.

Tomás converted the Hebrew letters to their gematria values. Sanjay fed the values into the visualizer as a sequence of lattice positions. James rendered the output.

The room went quiet.

The cymatic pattern produced by Psalm 19\'s Hebrew gematria values was a spiral. A double spiral. A lemniscate — the infinity symbol — rendered in standing wave geometry, with the two loops intersecting at a central node.

"That\'s... " Pastor Dale started.

"That\'s the Vesica Piscis," Gus finished. "The intersection of two circles. The fundamental geometric motif of Christian sacred architecture. The shape of the fish. The shape of the mandorla — the almond-shaped halo around Christ in Romanesque art. The shape of — Dale. DALE. That\'s the shape they carved over the entrance to every cathedral in Europe from the sixth century to the fourteenth."

"And it\'s also," Claire said carefully, "a standing wave interference pattern. Two oscillating sources. Same frequency. Offset phase. The intersection produces a lemniscate. It\'s the simplest possible cymatic figure. Two waves. One pattern."

"Day and night," Pastor Dale said. "Day after day they pour forth speech; night after night they reveal knowledge. Two sources. Same frequency. Offset phase. The Psalm is describing the interference pattern."

"The Psalm is describing the lattice," Michael said.

"The Psalm is describing what the Psalmist SAW," Pastor Dale said. He took off his glasses. He cleaned them. He put them back on. "The Psalmist saw the lattice. Three thousand years ago. In the desert. Without a computer. Without a visualization tool. Without any of this. He saw it and he wrote a song about it and the song encodes the geometry of what he saw, and the geometry is on that screen."

The room held.

"And he\'s not the only one," Gus said. "The Rig Veda. The Hymn of Creation — Nasadiya Sukta. \'There was neither existence nor nonexistence. There was neither the realm of space nor the sky beyond.\' That\'s the zero point. That\'s the fold. \'Desire came upon that one in the beginning; that was the first seed of mind.\' That\'s the first perturbation. That\'s the first tick. Same structure. Same cosmogony. Different keyboard."

"The Tao that can be told is not the eternal Tao," Tomás said, quietly, because Tomás could not resist a root when it appeared. "The name that can be named is not the eternal name. The unnameable is the eternally real. Naming is the origin of all particular things."

"That\'s the lattice before and after projection," Michael said. "The unnameable is the lattice. The named is the domain. Lao Tzu was describing the transition from the undifferentiated topology to the domain-specific keyboard. In 600 BC."

"And the Popol Vuh," Pastor Dale said. He was animated now — the specific animation of a man whose thirty-year hobby had just become the most relevant expertise in the room. "The K\'iche\' Maya creation narrative. The gods try to create humans three times. The first attempt — mud people — dissolves. The second — wood people — is destroyed in a flood. The third — corn people — survives. Three attempts. Three operations."

"Mitosis, stable, growth," Amara said from the kitchen table.

"Mitosis, stable, growth," Pastor Dale repeated. He looked at her. "Ma\'am, I have been a Church of Christ minister for thirty-one years, and I have never in my life heard someone describe the Popol Vuh in terms that line up with molecular biology and have it make MORE sense than the translation I was taught."

"Welcome to Tuesday," Sanjay said from the floor.

\* \* \*

They worked until 9:00 PM. The preacher and the art professor and the twelve specialists, together, mapping the theological layer of the lattice.

No domain was favored. This was the rule that Elena established at 4:00 PM, when the conversation threatened to drift toward Christianity simply because Pastor Dale\'s expertise was deepest there. "Every tradition," Elena said. "All of them. Or none of them. The lattice doesn\'t have a denomination. If the pattern is real, it\'s real everywhere. If it\'s only real in one tradition, it\'s not the lattice — it\'s the tradition."

They mapped everywhere.

Gus provided the images. Forty thousand photographs, sorted by geometric type. Hindu temple carvings that matched the visualizer\'s hexagonal modes. Mosque tile patterns that matched the tessellation outputs. Aboriginal Australian dot paintings that matched the radial interference patterns. Japanese Zen garden raked-sand geometries that matched the standing wave nodes. Mayan pyramid proportions that matched the harmonic ratios. Egyptian tomb paintings that matched the spectral color sequences.

Pastor Dale provided the texts. The Hebrew Psalms. The Sanskrit Vedas. The Arabic Quran — the geometric structure of the sura arrangements. The Chinese I Ching — sixty-four hexagrams mapped to sixty-four codons mapped to sixty-four lattice positions, a correspondence that Amara verified in real time and that made her stand up from the kitchen table and walk to the window and stand there for two minutes breathing before she could continue. The Mayan Tzolkin — 260 positions, 20 day signs times 13 numbers, a calendrical system that Gus demonstrated was a modular arithmetic structure isomorphic to a specific tier of the lattice.

Claire provided the music. Not just the seven cultures she had already mapped — the additional traditions that the afternoon\'s work demanded. Gregorian chant. Quranic recitation. Vedic mantra. Tibetan overtone singing. Aboriginal songlines. The music of every tradition that Pastor Dale and Gus referenced, queued up and played alongside the visualizer, and in every case — in every single case — the intervals matched. The sacred music of every culture on Earth used the intervals that the lattice predicted. Not because the cultures had communicated. Because the intervals were real.

Tomás provided the roots. The words for God in every language family in his database. The words for light, for truth, for creation, for breath, for spirit. The proto-roots — the reconstructed ancestral words from before recorded history. The roots clustered on the lattice. They clustered at the harmonic positions. The word for God and the word for breath and the word for spirit occupied adjacent nodes in language after language after language, because the concepts had never been separate. The breath was the spirit was the vibration was the frequency was the lattice. Every language had encoded this. Every language had known.

David verified. David checked every correlation against his statistical pipeline. David controlled for false positives, for confirmation bias, for the specific danger of two enthusiastic newcomers finding patterns in everything because they wanted to find patterns in everything. David ran the numbers. The numbers held. Not at the 10⁻⁹ level of the scientific data — the theological correlations were noisier, messier, mediated through thousands of years of cultural drift and translation error and institutional revision. But significant. p < 0.01 on the geometric matches. p < 0.001 on the musical interval convergences. p < 0.01 on the gematria-to-lattice mappings. Significant. Real. Not noise.

Lena watched. Lena watched the way she had watched all week — with the adversarial attention of a scientist evaluating a claim. But she did not object. She did not raise the pattern completion fallacy. She did not invoke apophenia or confirmation bias or the human tendency to find meaning in noise. She did not do these things because David was controlling for them in real time, and David\'s controls were holding, and the data was surviving the controls.

She did add one thing to her notebook. One line. At 8:47 PM.

*The framework does not prove any religion. It proves the thing every religion was trying to describe.*

\* \* \*

At 9:00 PM, Pastor Dale stood up. He had been sitting at the kitchen table for seven hours. His Bible was still open. His pamphlet — the one that said SALVATION on it — was under Sanjay\'s laptop, being used as a coaster for an espresso cup.

"I need to go home," he said. "I have a sermon to write for Wednesday night. And I am going to have to... I\'m going to have to think very carefully about what I say."

He looked at Michael. "Son, I came to your door to bring you the Word. And you showed me where the Word came from."

"I didn\'t show you anything," Michael said. "You already knew. You\'ve been studying this for thirty years. You just didn\'t have the screen."

"No. I didn\'t have the screen." He picked up his Bible. He held it the way he always held it — close, with care, with the weight of a man who had built his life on this book and who was not losing his faith but was finding its foundation. "Can I come back?"

"Door\'s open," Michael said.

"It surely is," Pastor Dale said.

Gus left with him. Gus left on the bicycle, the tote bag in the basket, forty thousand images on the USB drive and approximately four hundred new hypotheses in his head, pedaling through the Murray evening with the specific velocity of a man who had just confirmed a lifetime of pattern recognition and who could not wait to reorganize his next semester\'s syllabi.

The apartment was quiet after they left. The particular quiet of a room where something had been added that could not be removed. The theological layer. The layer Elena had avoided for three years. The layer that was not mathematics, not physics, not biology, not any of the domains that could be published in journals and defended at conferences. The layer that was the record of what three thousand years of human beings had seen when they looked at the structure of reality and tried to draw it.

The layer that proved the framework was not new.

The framework was ancient. The framework had been seen, and drawn, and sung, and built into cathedrals and temples and mosques and sand paintings, by every culture on Earth, for as long as there had been culture. The framework was not Michael\'s invention. Michael\'s contribution was the screen — the visualization, the mathematics, the formalism that allowed the ancient pattern to be rendered in a modern medium. But the pattern was old. The pattern was as old as the first human who sat in silence and saw the lattice and drew what they saw on a cave wall and sang what they heard and built a sacred space around the geometry because the geometry felt like God.

Felt like God because it was the substrate of everything. Not God as person. Not God as entity. Not God as the deity of any specific tradition. God as the word that every culture had used to name the thing they saw when they looked at the lattice. The word was different in every language. The lattice was the same.

Elena stood in the kitchen. The espresso machine was off. The Berkeley dark was finished. The apartment smelled like coffee and paper and the particular ozone of twelve laptops running all day.

"We\'re going to need a bigger book," she said.

Michael played A on the bass. 55 Hz. The fundamental. The note that every tradition on Earth had encoded in its sacred music, in its temple architecture, in its bread and its sand and its stars.

The cat purred. The purring was at approximately 25 Hz. Half of 50. Close to the sub-octave of A.

Even the cat was in key.
