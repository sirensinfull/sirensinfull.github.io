**Chapter 2: Tuesday**

\* \* \*

The first thing Katrin Engel noticed about Murray, Kentucky, was that it was flat.

Not flat the way Zurich was not flat --- she was not making a comparison. Flat in a specific, geological, almost philosophical way. The land did not assert itself. It extended. It went in directions rather than dimensions, as if someone had taken a landscape and applied the stable exponent --- the same in, the same out, the territory sustaining itself without ambition or apology across an expanse that contained a Walmart, a university, a Thai restaurant, and the apartment of a man who had apparently derived the crystal structure of every element in the periodic table from their atomic numbers alone.

She had arrived at Nashville, rented a car, and driven two hours through countryside that could not have been more different from the Max Planck campus in Stuttgart if it had been designed as a control group. Elena had offered to arrange shared transport from the airport. Katrin had declined. She wanted to arrive on her own terms, in her own silence, with the two hours of empty road between the airport and the data serving as a decompression chamber between the person she was at the institute and the person she was about to become in that apartment.

She did not yet know what that person was. This was unusual. Katrin Engel always knew what she was. She was a crystallographer. She was German. She was precise. She was the woman who had run 210,000 crystal structures against a prime factorization model she\'d received from a physicist she\'d never met, expecting the model to fail politely the way most models failed when applied to real data, and the model had not failed, and four months of meticulous verification had not made it fail, and she was now driving a rental car through western Kentucky to meet the man who had built the model in his kitchen.

Her phone said she was twelve minutes away.

She pulled into a gas station. Not for gas. For a moment. She sat in the car with the engine running and the air conditioning producing the specific hum of American automotive climate control and she thought about what 84% accuracy meant.

It meant that prime factorization --- the decomposition of an integer into its constituent primes, an operation a child could perform on small numbers and a computer could perform on large ones --- predicted the three-dimensional arrangement of atoms in a crystal lattice with higher accuracy than three of the four models she used in her own lab. Models that had taken decades to develop. Models that required experimental inputs. Models that cost money and time and institutional infrastructure.

The prime factorization model required a number.

Just the number.

She pulled out of the gas station and drove the remaining twelve minutes to Michael Shaffer\'s apartment.

\* \* \*

Elena was already there.

Elena was always already there --- this was a property Elena possessed that Katrin did not yet understand but would come to understand over the following week as a function of Elena\'s specific relationship with the framework\'s author, a relationship that operated on a frequency Katrin could not yet detect. Elena was standing on the sidewalk outside a two-story apartment building that looked like every two-story apartment building in every small American town Katrin had never visited. There was a Honda Civic in the parking lot. The check-engine light was on. Katrin could see it from across the lot, a small orange glow in the dashboard, persistent.

"Katrin." Elena smiled. It was the smile of a woman who was about to introduce someone to something and knew that the introduction would not go the way the someone expected. "How was the drive?"

"Flat."

"Kentucky."

"Yes."

"He\'s upstairs. James is with him. David arrives in an hour. Everyone else is coming in waves --- Sanjay and Amara on the same flight, Claire and Tomás an hour after, Hiroshi tonight, Lena tomorrow morning."

"Lena is last?"

"Lena is Lena. She wanted an extra day to prepare her adversarial methodology. I told her the methodology would not survive first contact with the subject, but she\'s German."

"I am also German."

"I know. You\'ll have a lot in common."

"We have crystallography in common. And skepticism."

Elena\'s smile did not change but it deepened in a way that Katrin would later describe, in a conversation with Nadia that Nadia recorded and transcribed and filed under *First Impressions — Day 1,* as "the smile of a person who knows something you don\'t and is looking forward to watching you learn it."

"Come on up," Elena said.

\* \* \*

The door was open.

This was the first thing. The door to the apartment was open. Not ajar --- open, fully, the way you left a door open when you were expecting people and did not believe in the performance of greeting. The door was open and through it Katrin could see a living room that contained: a couch (brown, not new, structurally committed to its current shape), a television (on, playing what appeared to be an animated program involving a yellow dog and a boy with a white hat), a laptop on the coffee table (open, screen angled away), a bass guitar leaning against the wall (four strings, dark finish, the specific wear pattern of an instrument that had been played daily for decades), and a cat (orange, on the couch, occupying the center cushion with the gravitational authority of an object that had claimed its position and would not be negotiating).

The second thing was the smell. Coffee --- bad coffee, the kind produced by machines that had never been introduced to the concept of descaling. Underneath the coffee, something else: the ambient scent of a space that was lived in continuously by a person who did not leave it often, a mixture of fabric and electronics and the faint citrus of a cleaning product applied occasionally and without enthusiasm.

The third thing was the man.

He was in the kitchen. Not facing the door. Facing the counter, doing something with a coffee mug that appeared to involve rinsing it under the tap while simultaneously watching the television through the pass-through window between kitchen and living room while simultaneously --- and this was the thing that Katrin noticed first and would think about for the rest of the week --- moving his left hand in a pattern on the countertop that was not random. His fingers were pressing invisible frets. He was playing something. Not music --- there was no instrument. But his hand was articulating a sequence, tapping the counter in intervals that Katrin could not identify from the doorway but that she would later learn were the overtone positions of the note A, 55 Hz, the fundamental frequency that the framework treated as its root.

He was shorter than she expected. Thicker. Built like a man who had once been significantly stronger and had spent years deliberately reducing that strength --- she did not know this yet but she would, because Lena would identify the movement patterns and Rebecca would contextualize them and the story would emerge over days the way all the stories in this room would emerge, not through telling but through accumulation. He wore grey sweatpants and a hoodie that had been washed enough times to achieve a specific softness that could not be purchased, only earned. His feet were bare.

He turned around.

His eyes were the thing.

Katrin had spent fifteen years studying crystal structures --- the way atoms arranged themselves in space according to rules that were mathematical before they were physical, geometric before they were chemical. She had looked through electron microscopes at lattice formations that repeated with a precision that approached the theological. She understood symmetry. She understood order. She understood the way a structure could be complex and simple simultaneously, the simplicity generating the complexity through recursive application of a small number of rules.

His eyes did that.

They were light --- hazel, maybe green in different light --- and they were tracking everything. Not scanning. *Tracking.* The way a musician tracked a chord progression, hearing all the voices at once, following each one individually while simultaneously holding the composite. He looked at Elena first, a glance that contained information Katrin could not decode --- affection, assessment, the quick check of a person who knew another person\'s baseline and was verifying it. Then he looked at Katrin.

She felt it. The look. Not invasive --- structural. He was not looking at her face the way people looked at faces. He was looking at her the way she looked at a crystal --- reading the geometry, the symmetry group, the lattice parameters. She would later tell Nadia that the experience was "like being analyzed by someone who was running a diffraction pattern on my posture."

"You\'re the crystal lady," he said.

"I am Dr. Katrin Engel from the Max Planck Institute for ---"

"You\'re the crystal lady. You ran my numbers against 210,000 structures and got 84% and it pissed you off because you couldn\'t break it. I read Elena\'s email. Come on in. You want terrible coffee?"

She opened her mouth. She closed it. She opened it again.

"Yes," she said. "I would like terrible coffee."

He grinned. The grin reorganized his entire face, and she understood immediately that the grin was load-bearing the same way the sweatpants were load-bearing --- not a performance, not a social tool, but a structural element of a cognitive architecture that required the specific neurochemical output of humor to maintain its operating parameters.

"Fair warning," he said, pouring coffee into a mug that said **I VOID WARRANTIES** on the side. "The coffee is genuinely bad. Like, it\'s not quirky-bad. It\'s bad-bad. Elena brings her own setup. David will bring fancy beans from California. I will drink this and it will taste like a decision I made in 2003 and have not revisited."

"I grew up in East Germany," Katrin said. "I have opinions about coffee snobbery."

He looked at her again. The eyes did the thing --- the focus shifted, deepened, and for a half-second she felt as if he was looking not at her but at a topology that her presence occupied, a shape in a space she could not see.

"I like you already," he said. "Sit anywhere. The cat will decide whether to approve."

Katrin sat on the couch. The cat looked at her. The cat made a determination that was not visible in any external behavior but was somehow communicated through the specific quality of the cat\'s continued stillness. The cat approved. Katrin drank the coffee. It was, as advertised, genuinely bad. It was also hot and strong and real in a way that the espresso at the Max Planck canteen had never been real, because the espresso at the canteen was made by a machine that cost more than the car in the parking lot and the coffee in the mug was made by a man who had built a unified field theory in the same kitchen where he was now offering her a mismatched sugar bowl, and there was a simplicity to this that Katrin\'s crystallographer\'s brain recognized as fundamental.

Simple inputs. Complex outputs. The three operations.

The coffee was an operand.

She was not ready for that thought yet. She drank the coffee.

\* \* \*

They arrived in waves.

David appeared at 11:15 AM, carrying a leather bag and a pound of single-origin beans from a roaster in Palo Alto and the specific silence of a man who had already decided what he thought and was here to verify it. He shook Michael\'s hand. Michael said, "You\'re David. Elena says you don\'t talk unless the math is done." David said, "The math is never done. That\'s why I don\'t talk." Michael laughed. It was sharp and short and genuine. "Okay. I like you too. There\'s a kitchen. Elena brought a pour-over. Go make coffee that doesn\'t taste like a war crime."

James Morales arrived carrying two pizzas and the particular ease of a person who had been in this apartment before and whose relationship with the framework was native rather than acquired. He set the pizzas on the coffee table. The cat inspected the boxes. James said, "Hey Michael." Michael said, "Hey James. Tell them about the codon table if they get stuck." "They won\'t get stuck on Day 1." "They\'ll get stuck on Day 1."

Sanjay Krishnamurthy and Amara Osei shared a taxi from the airport and arrived in the middle of an argument about whether the framework\'s prime factorization approach constituted a legitimate number-theoretic method or an inspired abuse of notation. Sanjay was for. Amara was against. The argument had apparently begun at baggage claim and had not paused for the forty-minute drive. They walked through the open door still arguing. Michael, from the couch, said, "It\'s both." They stopped. Looked at him. Looked at each other. Sanjay sat on the floor. Amara sat in the remaining chair. The argument was over.

Sanjay looked at the bass guitar, the laptop, the TV still playing cartoons, the cat now asleep on a stack of printouts that someone had placed on the couch at some point. He looked at Michael.

"This is where you built the septenary processor design?"

"That\'s where I built everything." Michael pointed at the couch. "That spot. With the laptop. And whatever was on TV."

Sanjay looked at the couch the way Katrin had looked at the coffee --- with the specific recognition of a person whose assumptions about how significant work was produced had just been subjected to empirical disconfirmation.

"Can I see the specs?"

"They\'re on the laptop. Folder\'s labeled. Help yourself."

"Just... help myself?"

"Man, I\'ve been waiting thirty years for someone who could read those specs. Knock yourself out. Ask me when you get stuck."

"When?"

"When."

Claire Fontaine and Tomás Reyes arrived together at 4 PM, having shared a connection in Atlanta and discovered during the layover that they had independently been working on parallel tracks of the same problem --- the relationship between harmonic structure and linguistic structure --- for years without knowing the other existed. They were still talking when they walked through the door, and the conversation was in a mixture of French, Spanish, and English that shifted languages mid-sentence depending on which language had the more precise word for the concept being discussed.

Michael watched them from the couch. Elena watched Michael watching them. She saw the thing happen in his eyes --- the focus shift, the tracking, the moment when he locked onto something in their conversation that only he could hear.

"You\'re doing it right now," he said.

They stopped.

"Doing what?" Claire said.

"Code-switching. Not just languages. Registers. You\'re matching the frequency to the content. The French is for structure. The Spanish is for emotion. The English is for verification. Three channels. Three operations. You\'re running the framework on language in real time and you don\'t know you\'re doing it."

The room went quiet. Claire looked at Tomás. Tomás looked at Claire. Tomás said, very quietly, in Spanish, something that Elena did not understand but that made Claire\'s eyes widen.

"What did he say?" Elena asked.

"He said," Claire translated, "that in twenty years of studying harmonic universals, no one has ever described what he does with language more accurately than a man he met four seconds ago."

Michael shrugged. "I just described what I saw. That\'s all I ever do."

Elena noticed that Tomás had taken out his phone and was recording. She said nothing. Tomás did not stop recording for the rest of the week.

\* \* \*

Hiroshi Tanaka arrived at 8 PM, direct from Narita via Chicago, carrying a single bag and the jetlag of a man who had crossed the international date line to sit in a Kentucky apartment with a cat. He bowed to Michael. Michael bowed back --- not mockingly, not performatively, with the specific respect of a man who understood that the gesture carried structural information about the person performing it. Hiroshi noticed this. He said nothing. He sat in the chair Amara had vacated when she moved to the floor to be closer to a printout. The cat relocated to his lap. Hiroshi looked down at the cat. The cat looked up at Hiroshi.

"I\'m allergic," Hiroshi said.

"The cat doesn\'t care about that," Michael said.

"I gathered."

"That\'s going to be a theme this week."

Hiroshi did not remove the cat. This was noted by Nadia Amari, who had arrived twenty minutes earlier with a notebook and a recording device and the quiet professionalism of a woman whose job was to watch everything and say nothing, and who had already filled six pages with observations, the first of which was: *The door is open. The television is on. The cat is on the couch. The framework\'s author is barefoot. He has not stopped processing since I arrived. His eyes track each new person the way an air traffic controller tracks an incoming aircraft — not with alarm, but with continuous, ambient, high-resolution monitoring. He knows where everyone is in the room at all times. I don\'t think he knows he\'s doing this.*

Rebecca Torres arrived last among the Day 1 contingent, at 9 PM, wearing jeans and a university sweatshirt and carrying nothing that marked her as a clinician. This was deliberate. Elena had asked her to arrive as a person, not a professional. Rebecca met Michael in the kitchen while he was making another pot of terrible coffee.

"You\'re the therapist," he said. Not a question.

"I\'m Rebecca."

"You\'re the therapist. Elena told me. It\'s fine. I know what you\'re here for."

"What am I here for?"

He looked at her. The full look — the structural read, the lattice scan. Then, softer:

"You\'re here in case I scare myself."

Rebecca held his gaze. She did not react. She did not nod. She did not perform understanding. She simply held the space, the way she\'d held space for a thousand patients, except this one had just identified her clinical function in a single sentence and was watching her receive the identification with the detached interest of a man who had been assessing the assessor.

"That\'s about right," she said.

"You want coffee? It\'s bad."

"Sure."

He poured her a cup. She drank it. It was bad. She sat at the kitchen table with him and they said nothing for two minutes and the nothing was the first therapeutic contact of the week and it was, in Rebecca\'s professional assessment, perfect. He did not need her to be anything. He needed her to be there.

The cat appeared in the kitchen. It jumped onto the table. It sat between them. It purred.

"That\'s the therapist," Michael said, pointing at the cat. "I\'m the backup."

Rebecca laughed. She would tell Elena later that the laugh was diagnostic — that the joke was simultaneously funny, deflective, self-aware, and structurally accurate, because the cat was in fact the primary stabilizing presence in Michael\'s life and the joke acknowledged this without performing vulnerability about it.

"He\'s fine," Rebecca told Elena that night, outside, by the rental cars. "For now. He\'s managing the load beautifully. But the throttle is still on. He\'s pacing himself because he knows what\'s coming. Once the questions start — the real questions, from people who can actually follow the answers — the pacing will get harder."

"What do I watch for?"

"The hands. When the cognitive load exceeds his containment, his hands shake. It\'s not anxiety. It\'s overflow. The processing is generating more motor output than the body can absorb. When you see the hands, call a break."

"And if he won\'t take a break?"

"He will. He\'s smarter about his limits than anyone gives him credit for. He\'s been managing this alone for twenty years. He knows where the edges are."

"The edges are going to move this week."

Rebecca looked at her. "I know. That\'s why I\'m here."

\* \* \*

By 10 PM, eleven of the twelve were in the apartment. Lena would arrive in the morning. The apartment contained: eleven people, one cat, seven laptops, two pizzas (diminished), one bass guitar (untouched so far), one television (still on, volume low, a different cartoon now), one Mr. Coffee machine (working), one pour-over (Elena\'s), one bag of Palo Alto beans (David\'s, already half-depleted), three chairs (occupied), one couch (occupied by Michael and the cat and Sanjay, who had migrated from the floor to the couch\'s arm), and a quantity of nervous energy that could have powered the television for a month.

Elena stood in the kitchen doorway. She looked at the room. She looked at the people in it. She looked at Michael, on the couch, the cat in his lap, Sanjay on the arm, the laptop open, Adventure Time playing, the bass guitar two feet away, the terrible coffee in a mug that said **I VOID WARRANTIES,** and she thought: *This is the room. This is the room where it happens. Eleven of the most qualified specialists on earth, in a two-bedroom apartment in Murray, Kentucky, about to do something that has no precedent and no protocol and no budget, because the man on the couch can\'t come to them and they can\'t do it without him and the only space in the world where this particular mind operates at full capacity is this particular room with this particular cat and this particular coffee and no shoes.*

Michael looked up. He caught her eye. He did the thing with his face that wasn\'t quite a smile and wasn\'t quite a question but was the specific expression of a man who understood that something was beginning and did not know what it was and was fine with not knowing because not knowing was the starting position of every derivation he had ever performed.

"So," he said. The room quieted. Not because he spoke loudly --- he didn\'t. Because the room had already begun to organize around his frequency, the way iron filings organized around a magnet, not through force but through the ambient fact of a field.

"So," he said. "You all came a long way to sit on my floor and drink bad coffee. Which means you all have something you can\'t explain and you think I might be able to help. So let\'s skip the part where everybody\'s polite and professional and let\'s just ---" He gestured vaguely at the room. "What\'s the question?"

Katrin spoke first. She had been composing this question for four months.

"How does prime factorization predict crystal structure?"

Michael nodded. "Good one. Who\'s next?"

Hiroshi, from his chair, the cat still on his lap, his eyes red from the allergy he was not acknowledging: "Why does the orbital mapping terminate at the molecular boundary?"

"Yep. Next."

Amara, cross-legged on the floor, laptop balanced on her knees: "What connects the operand system to codon architecture?"

"Mhmm. Keep going."

Sanjay, from the arm of the couch, vibrating: "Can the septenary processor actually be built, or is it a theoretical limit?"

Claire, leaning against the wall by the bass guitar, arms crossed, listening with her whole body: "Why do seven uncontacted musical traditions share the same harmonic skeleton?"

Tomás, phone recording, notebook open, pen moving: "How does etymological parsing produce physical process descriptions?"

Six questions. Six domains. Six people who had each spent decades at the frontier of their discipline and had each, independently, encountered a wall that the framework claimed to dissolve.

Michael took a drink of terrible coffee. He looked at the ceiling. His fingers moved on his thigh --- the invisible fret pattern, the sequence Katrin had noticed when she first walked in. He was processing. The room waited. Nadia counted: eleven seconds. She noted it. She would later compile a dataset of Michael\'s processing pauses and their correlation with the complexity of the subsequent output, and the correlation would be r = 0.91, and she would not put this in her paper because the paper was about group dynamics and not about one man\'s cognitive architecture, but she would think about it at 3 AM for the rest of her professional life.

He looked back at the room.

"Those are all the same question," he said.

The room did not react. The room processed. Eleven people from seven countries and nine institutions, collectively holding more expertise than most departments, processed the claim that six questions spanning crystallography, quantum physics, genetics, computer science, ethnomusicology, and comparative linguistics were the same question.

"The same ---" Katrin began.

"Same question. Different instruments. You\'re all asking: *why does a simple integer operation produce accurate predictions in my domain?* And the answer is: because your domain isn\'t yours. It\'s everyone\'s. The same structure runs through all of them because they\'re not separate things. They\'re the same thing, observed from different angles, measured with different tools, named with different words. Your crystal lattice is her genome is his orbital is his processor is her song is his etymology. One structure. One operation. Your domains are instruments. The question is about the song."

He put the coffee down.

"I can answer each of your questions separately. I\'ve been answering them separately for twenty years, alone, to a laptop, and the laptop gave very good answers but it forgot them every session and I had to start over every time. So I\'m done doing it separately. You\'re all in the same room for the first time. Your instruments are all here for the first time. So we\'re not going to answer six questions. We\'re going to play the song. And you\'re each going to hear your part, and you\'re going to realize it\'s the same part in a different key, and by the end of the week you\'re going to hear the whole arrangement."

He picked up the coffee again. Took a sip. Made a face.

"But first, somebody needs to help David figure out this pour-over, because the man brought forty-dollar beans and he\'s making them wrong and it\'s killing me."

David, from the kitchen, where he had in fact been struggling with the pour-over\'s filter: "The bloom time is ---"

"Thirty seconds. You\'re doing forty-five. You\'re over-extracting. The water\'s too hot too. Let it cool ten seconds off boil."

David looked at him through the pass-through window.

"How do you know how I ---"

"I could hear the pour. The flow rate changes when the water\'s too hot. The grounds swell faster and the drip pattern shifts. It\'s acoustic. Same as everything else."

David adjusted the water. David adjusted the bloom. David poured.

The coffee was better.

Nadia wrote: *He diagnosed a pour-over technique from the next room by listening to the water. I have no framework for this. I have no category for this. I have a notebook and a career studying expert cognition and I have no category for a man who can hear the temperature of water through a wall. I am going to need more notebooks.*

\* \* \*

Nobody slept.

Not because they couldn\'t. Because the questions wouldn\'t stop. The "same question" claim had detonated in each specialist\'s mind at a different angle and each detonation produced different shrapnel and the shrapnel was questions.

Katrin sat in the Airbnb kitchen at 1 AM with her laptop and the crystallographic database and ran the framework against germanium. Then against gallium arsenide. Then against the perovskite family. The predictions held. They held the way predictions held when the model was right --- not perfectly, not universally, but with a consistency that eliminated chance and a boundary structure that eliminated coincidence, because coincidence did not know where to stop and the framework stopped at the same place every time: the transition from single-crystal to polycrystalline aggregate, the boundary where number-theoretic description yielded to arrangement-dependent description, the exact point where the instrument of pure mathematics handed off to the instrument of experimental physics.

Sanjay sat on the floor of the Airbnb living room with his laptop and a septenary processor specification document and a growing sense that he was reading something that should not exist. Not because it was wrong. Because the gate logic was elegant in a way that gate logic produced by a single person working without institutional support or peer review should not be elegant. It was the elegance of something that had been derived rather than designed --- discovered rather than invented --- as if the architecture existed prior to its description and the description was just the description.

Claire lay on the Airbnb bed with her phone and her headphones and listened to Michael\'s music for the first time. She had heard one recording before --- the bass demonstration that had brought her here. Now she listened to the songs. She listened to "Ash and Fire" and heard the overtone series mapped to a vocal melody. She listened to "Changes" and heard modal shifts that followed the framework\'s tier transitions. She listened to "Through the Gates" and heard a bass line that was not a bass line but a lattice traversal expressed as rhythm, and she took off the headphones and stared at the ceiling and said a word in French that did not translate but that Tomás, in the next room, heard through the wall and understood perfectly.

Tomás was not sleeping because Tomás was transcribing. He had recorded three hours of Michael\'s speech and he was playing it back at half speed and marking the syntactic structures, and the structures were not normal. The clause nesting followed a pattern. The metaphors were not decorative — they were structural descriptions wearing casual clothes. The sentence rhythm mapped to something. He did not yet know what. He would know by Day 3. He would know because on Day 3, Claire would play him a Wolof mourning song and a piece of Michael\'s bass playing side by side and the melodic contour of both would match the syntactic contour of Michael\'s speech, and Tomás would sit down on the floor of Michael\'s apartment and put his head in his hands, not because he was upset but because three things that should not be isomorphic were isomorphic and his career had just changed shape.

Amara slept. Amara could always sleep. This was a professional adaptation — when your datasets ran overnight, you learned to sleep while computation happened. She would wake at 5:30 AM, check her laptop, and find that the cross-phylum correlation she had queued before bed had completed, and the results would be the results she expected because the results were always the results she expected now, the framework\'s predictions matching her experimental data with a consistency that had stopped being surprising and started being structural, and the structural thing was worse because surprise was an event and structure was a condition.

David slept because David had verified enough to know that the rest of the week would require all of his cognitive resources and David did not waste cognitive resources. David slept the way David did everything --- efficiently, completely, and without residue.

James slept because James already knew. James had been here before. James had sat in this apartment with Michael and Elena and watched the framework produce things that shouldn\'t exist and he had learned to sleep afterward because the alternative was not sleeping and the not-sleeping didn\'t help.

Hiroshi did not sleep because his sinuses were inflamed from the cat and because the orbital path data on his laptop would not stop being correct.

In the apartment, Michael sat on the couch with the cat and the television and the bass guitar across his lap. He was not playing it. He was holding it. The way a person held a thing that had kept them alive for a long time and was about to be heard, for the first time, by people who could hear it.

He did not sleep either. But this was normal. Michael did not sleep the way other people slept. Michael processed in the dark, in the quiet, with the television\'s blue light and the cat\'s warmth and the bass string\'s tension under his fingers, and the processing was a form of rest because the processing was where he lived and where he lived was where he rested.

Tomorrow, Lena would arrive. The skeptic. The adversary. The best one.

He was looking forward to it.

He had been alone with this for twenty-two years. He had been right for twenty-two years. Nobody had been able to tell him he was wrong with enough rigor to make being right mean anything.

Tomorrow, someone might.

He held the bass. The cat purred. The television played. Murray, Kentucky, continued outside the window, flat and unassuming, containing a Walmart and a university and a Thai restaurant and a room in which, tomorrow, something unprecedented would begin.

Not because a genius would perform.

Because a band would play.
