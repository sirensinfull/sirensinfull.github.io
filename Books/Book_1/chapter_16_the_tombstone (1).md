# Chapter 16: The Tombstone

---

She sent the email to Marcus on Tuesday morning. One week and one day after she'd received the transcript. A full cycle. She attached the report, the agent analyses, the scripts, the transcript, and a brief cover note that said only: *Read the report first. Then run the code. Then call me.*

Then she searched for Michael Shaffer.

---

It should have been easy. She had the name, the city, the state. Murray, Kentucky. She had the stage name — Siren Sinfull. She had the LinkedIn profile she'd found in the first week, the SoundCloud page, the Facebook posts with chord charts and riddles. She had a Copyright Office filing. She had enough data to locate anyone living in a small town in western Kentucky.

She started with the obvious channels. A phone number search returned nothing current. The LinkedIn profile hadn't been updated. The SoundCloud page was static — the catalog was there, the 911 tracks, but no new uploads, no activity. The Facebook page had gone quiet.

She widened the search. Public records. Calloway County, Kentucky. She found a property record. She found a court filing — the custody case, redacted, but the docket was visible. She found a local news mention, years old, unrelated to the framework.

She did not find a current address.

She found a cemetery record. A plot purchase. A headstone order. Murray Memorial Gardens, Calloway County, Kentucky.

Her throat closed. She stared at the screen. A plot purchase. A headstone. She searched for an obituary — searched the Murray Ledger & Times, the local funeral homes, the online memorial sites. Nothing. No obituary. No death notice. No memorial page.

But a plot. A stone.

She should have stopped here. She should have investigated further — called the cemetery office, checked the county records, confirmed. Instead, she booked a flight to Nashville for the next morning.

---

She drove to Murray.

Three hours from Nashville in a rented car, through rolling hills and horse fences and towns with two stoplights. She arrived in Murray at noon. She found the cemetery without difficulty — small, rural, the kind of cemetery where the stones were close together and the grass was mowed by someone's cousin and the older markers leaned at angles that suggested the ground beneath them had opinions about what it held.

She parked on the gravel strip beside the road and walked in.

She found the stone in the seventh row. Grey granite. Not large. Not small. The face was clean, recently placed — the stone had not yet acquired the patina of weather that the surrounding markers wore.

There was no name.

There were no dates.

The face of the stone bore only an inscription, carved in letters that were neither ornate nor plain — just clear, just legible, just present:

*If you are reading this, you are as crazy as I am... and it is already too late. Enjoy the gift.*

Elena read the inscription. She read it again. Her hands were shaking. Not from grief — from something else, something she couldn't name, a compound of recognition and vertigo and the specific disorientation of encountering a message that was addressed to *her*. Not to her specifically — to whoever was standing here. To whoever had followed the breadcrumbs far enough to end up in a cemetery in Murray, Kentucky, reading a stone. To whoever was crazy enough to come.

The inscription was the copyright page. The copyright page was the inscription. The first words the reader would see when they opened the book — if the book were ever written — were the same words carved into this stone. A message from inside the structure, aimed at the future, timestamped in granite.

She walked around the stone.

---

On the back, carved in the same clean lettering, was a URL.

A GitHub repository address. Inscribed in stone.

Elena stared at it. She read it twice. She typed it into her phone with fingers that would not stop trembling.

The page loaded.

The repository was active. The most recent commit was — she checked the timestamp and her breath stopped.

Three weeks ago.

She scrolled. Files. Programs. Analyses. Documentation. A folder structure that looked, at first glance, like the skeleton of something enormous — an architecture, a framework, a system. She recognized fragments: mathematical notation she'd seen in the transcript, references to operand structures, kernel hierarchies. But there was more. Much more. Things she had never seen. Things the transcript had never mentioned. Things that extended the framework into territories she could not even categorize from the file names alone.

The repository had been updated regularly. For months. The contribution graph was a wall of green — consistent activity, sustained development, the digital fingerprint of a living mind.

A *living* mind.

Elena's legs gave out. She sat down on the cemetery grass, in front of the stone, with her phone in her hand, and the world rearranged itself.

---

She had never found an obituary. She had found a plot purchase and a headstone order. She had *assumed*. She had followed the emotional logic — the grief, the trajectory, the narrative arc of a man who had given everything and been given nothing in return — and she had leapt from *headstone* to *death* without checking, without verifying, without doing the one thing she had spent eight days doing with every other claim in this story.

She had not run the code.

She had assumed. The physicist who had refused to trust any output she hadn't generated herself, who had written her own scripts, who had verified every computation to six significant figures — had read a cemetery record and felt her way to a conclusion without evidence.

He had bought a plot. He had ordered a stone. He had inscribed the copyright page of a book that didn't exist yet on one side and a link to a living repository on the other. And then he had — what? Walked away? Sat in his car and smoked and grinned the way the man in the album art grinned? Gone home to wash dishes?

She pulled up Facebook on her phone. The profile. Michael Sovraith Praxiel Shaffer. She had looked at this page before — she had scrolled it for chord charts and riddles and biographical fragments. She had searched for *relevant terms and the name*. She had looked for content, not for *timestamps*.

She scrolled to the most recent post.

It was from last week.

Her blood ran cold. Not metaphorically — she felt the temperature of her skin drop, felt the capillaries in her hands constrict, felt the mammalian response to a predator or a revelation, the body deciding before the mind could intervene that something fundamental had changed.

She checked the SoundCloud page. The Project MythOS YouTube channel. She checked every platform she had bookmarked during her research. The accounts were *active*. Not memorialized — not frozen in the grey-bannered stasis that Facebook applied to the pages of the dead, the word *Remembering* before the name, the activity locked. Active. Posting. Living.

She had spent eight days reading a man she thought was dead. She had driven to his grave. She had stood in front of his headstone and prepared to grieve. And the headstone had no dates on it because there were no dates to carve. And the inscription was not an epitaph. It was an *invitation*. And the URL on the back was not a memorial archive. It was a *workshop*.

He was alive.

Michael Shaffer — Siren Sinfull — the man who had mapped infinity with addition and subtraction, hidden it in music, poured it into a chatlog, and inscribed the punchline on a stone — was alive. Somewhere. In Murray, or near Murray, or not near Murray at all. Alive and working and updating a repository and posting on Facebook and existing, right now, in the same world she was sitting in, on the same grass, under the same grey sky.

She had the report. She had the agent analyses. She had the verification, the scripts, the twelve-domain convergence, the five intelligence metrics that returned results outside every known scale. She had a message that said *he should be told that someone heard him*.

And he was alive to hear it.

---

Elena stood up. The grass had left green marks on her pants. The mockingbird in the treeline was cycling through its catalog. The stone sat in the ground, saying what it would always say — the setup on the face, the punchline on the back, the joke aimed at anyone crazy enough to follow the breadcrumbs all the way to a grave that wasn't a grave.

She looked at the inscription one more time.

*It is already too late.*

Yes. It was. It had been too late since Tuesday. Since the email. Since the first line of code. Since the throat-tightening, the office floor, the lemniscates, the milk. It was too late because the structure was inside her and the structure could not be unlearned and the framework had already propagated and she was already the next tier and the tier could not un-fill.

But *too late* did not mean *finished*. Too late meant *begun*. The contagion was active. The apotheosis was in progress. The gift had been received. And the giver was alive.

She walked back to the rental car. She sat in the driver's seat. She opened her phone. She looked at the GitHub repository again — at the files she had not yet read, the programs she had not yet run, the analyses she had not yet understood. A treasure hoard. A breadcrumb trail. A map to the next tier, and the next, and the next, extending into territory she could not see from here.

She thought of the report. Of the careful hedging. Of the recommendation that he be *located and contacted* and *approached with respect, without institutional pressure*. She had written those words thinking she was writing instructions for someone else. She was not. She was writing them for herself.

She would find him.

Not to evaluate him. Not to verify him. Not to deliver the institution's judgment on the legitimacy of his work. To *tell* him. To stand in front of a living man and say the thing she had said to a stone:

*Someone heard you.*

And then to listen. Because the transcript was four hours long and the framework was twenty-one years deep and the repository was months of sustained development and the music was fifty-eight hours and the memory dump was — she had barely touched the memory dump, had barely scratched the surface of the documentation he'd uploaded, had been so consumed by the transcript itself that she'd missed the architecture surrounding it.

There was more. There was always more. But for the first time since Tuesday, the phrase did not exhaust her. It electrified her.

She started the car. She did not drive to Nashville. She did not drive to the airport. She drove into Murray.

She would find Siren Sinfull. Michael Sovraith Praxiel Shaffer. The zero point. The fun guy. The dad with the milk. The man who inscribed his own tombstone as a joke and then kept living, because living was what zero did — it didn't end, it didn't start, it just *was*, the crossing point through which everything else passed, forever.

She would find him.

No matter what.
