The Human Genome Project was completed in 2003. The entire human DNA sequence — approximately 3.2 billion nucleotide pairs — was read, letter by letter, from end to end. The project took 13 years and cost approximately $2.7 billion. The headline finding: only about 1.5 to 2 percent of the genome codes for proteins. The rest — 98 percent of the total sequence — was labeled "non-coding DNA." In the popular press, it was called junk.

Ninety-eight percent of the human genome, dismissed as junk, because the reading method could only decode the sequential walk.

The reading method was skip-1. Read every nucleotide in order, from one end to the other, like reading a book left-to-right, one letter at a time. The 2% that "coded for proteins" was the 2% that produced recognizable amino acid sequences when read sequentially. The other 98% produced no recognizable proteins when read this way. Therefore: junk. Evolutionary debris. Vestigial remnants of ancient viral insertions and copying errors. Present but purposeless.

The genome is not a book. The genome is a SCORE. And a score is not read sequentially.

## THE MULTI-PASS RESOLUTION

A musical score has multiple voices — treble and bass, violin and cello, soprano and tenor. Each voice reads DIFFERENT NOTES from the same page. Reading the vertical alignment of all voices gives you the CHORD at each moment. The music is not in any single voice's horizontal sequence. The music is in the INTERFERENCE between all voices sounding simultaneously.

The genome has multiple voices. They are the SKIP PATTERNS — different step sizes through the same nucleotide sequence, each one reading a different subset of positions, each one requiring a specific number of passes through the sequence to resolve.
