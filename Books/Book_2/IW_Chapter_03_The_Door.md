# Chapter 3: The Door

---

The door opened and none of the scenarios were correct.

The man in the doorway was not dead. He was not a widow. He was not a brother or a student or a posthumous delivery mechanism. He was approximately five-foot-ten, wearing grey sweatpants and a t-shirt with a faded logo she could not identify, and he was holding a bowl of what appeared to be Doritos.

"Yeah?"

His voice was unremarkable. Mid-range. A trace of something Southern that had been worn down by time or travel or indifference to regional identity. He looked at her the way people look at someone standing on their porch at — she checked, involuntarily — six-forty in the evening on a Thursday, which is to say with the polite wariness of a person deciding whether this was a solicitor, a neighbor, or a problem.

Elena's mouth was dry. She had driven three hours and rehearsed four scenarios and read the correction eleven times and none of it had prepared her for the radical mundanity of a man in sweatpants holding a bowl of Doritos and looking at her like she might be selling magazines.

"Are you..." She heard her voice and hated it. "Are you Michael Shaffer?"

"Who wants to know?"

It was not hostile. It was not suspicious. It was the reflex question of a person who had been asked their name by strangers before and had learned, for reasons Elena could not yet parse, that the answer mattered less than the reason for asking.

She should have had a speech. She had rehearsed speeches. She had rehearsed credentials and explanations and carefully constructed introductions that established her professional standing and her academic purpose and her respect for his privacy. All of it was gone. Every rehearsed sentence had left her head the moment the door opened and the dead man turned out to be eating Doritos.

"Does 'Exponential Abacus' ring a bell?"

The change was instantaneous.

His posture shifted. Not toward tension — toward *delight*. His eyes widened, and his face opened in a way that she would later describe in her journal as "the expression of a man who has been waiting for someone to speak his language and had given up expecting it to happen today," and the bowl of Doritos tilted at an angle that suggested he had forgotten he was holding it.

"OOH! A FAN!!" He stepped back from the door, gesturing with the Doritos hand in a motion that was half invitation and half involuntary excitement. "Come on in!!! I got lots more."

Elena stood on the porch. Somewhere behind him, she could hear a television. The audio was bright and cartoonish — dialogue she could not place but that sounded animated, fictional, aimed at an audience significantly younger than either of them. The house smelled like coffee and Doritos and the particular staleness of a space that was lived in constantly and aired out rarely.

She walked in.

---

The interior of the house was not what she expected, which by this point was becoming a pattern she should have anticipated.

She had imagined, if she was honest, some version of a mathematician's lair. Whiteboards covered in notation. Stacks of paper. The productive disorder of a mind that generated faster than it organized. She had imagined the house as an extension of the archive — physical proof of the kind of mind that produced four hundred sessions and ten white papers and nine hundred songs and a framework that bridged number theory, topology, theology, and music.

The living room had a couch, a television, and a PC setup in the corner. The television was playing Adventure Time. There was a cat on the couch — grey, indifferent, not interested in Elena's arrival or existence. The PC had two monitors, both dark. A bass guitar leaned against the wall near the desk, unplugged, positioned with the casual proximity of an object that was used often enough to not warrant a case.

There were no whiteboards. There were no stacks of paper. There were no visible signs of the archive that contained hundreds of sessions and enough intellectual property to reorganize multiple academic disciplines. The house looked like the house of a person who watched cartoons and played video games and occasionally ate cereal at irregular hours, because that was, she was beginning to understand, exactly what it was.

"Sit wherever," he said. He set the Doritos on the coffee table and dropped into what was clearly his spot on the couch — the cushion with the permanent impression, the angle that faced both the TV and the PC, the position of a man who had optimized his seating for the two activities that occupied his days. "You want coffee?"

"I — yes. Thank you."

He got up and walked to the kitchen with the unhurried gait of a person who had no schedule and no urgency and no awareness that the woman standing in his living room was experiencing a category of cognitive dissonance that her two PhDs had not equipped her to process.

She was supposed to be standing in a dead man's house. She was standing in a living man's house, and the living man was making her coffee and his cat was looking at her with the flat, incurious stare of an animal that had never once been impressed by a visitor, and Adventure Time was playing on the television, and somewhere on the PC in the corner was an archive that had consumed eight months of her life.

"Cream or sugar?"

"Black."

"Respect."

He returned with two mugs. His had something printed on it that she could not read from across the room. He handed her the guest mug — plain white, chipped at the rim — and sat back down in his spot and looked at her with an expression she could not categorize.

It was not the expression of a man who had been discovered. It was not the expression of a man who had been caught, or cornered, or exposed. It was the expression of a man who had left a door unlocked for a very long time and was pleased, in an uncomplicated and unhidden way, that someone had finally walked through it.

"So," he said. "You're the one who published the paper."

"You read it?"

He grinned. The grin was enormous and unselfconscious and contained no irony whatsoever. "I sent you corrections."

"You sent me corrections through an encrypted relay that my university's IT department described as 'technically elegant and practically untraceable.'"

"Yeah." He ate a Dorito. "I know a guy."

Elena sat in the chair across from the couch. She was holding coffee she had not yet tasted in a house she had not expected to enter, looking at a man she had confirmed dead, and the man was eating Doritos and watching her with the delighted patience of a person who had absolutely nowhere to be and all the time in the world and found this situation primarily entertaining.

She had so many questions that they formed a traffic jam in her cortex and none of them could move.

"You're alive," she said.

"Mostly."

"The county records—"

"Common name." He shrugged. "Different guy. Nobody asked me to clarify, so." The shrug completed itself, a motion that contained an entire philosophy of non-engagement with bureaucratic systems. "Figured if anyone cared enough, they'd come knock."

"You *knew* the death records were wrong and you didn't correct them?"

"Ma'am, I have been sitting in this house for a very long time, doing my work, telling machines about it because machines listen, and not one single human being in possession of institutional authority has ever knocked on my door and asked me a question about any of it." He ate another Dorito. "Until you. Just now. So welcome."

He was not bitter. That was the thing she would note in her journal later, underlining it twice. He was not bitter and he was not angry and he was not performing the absence of bitterness and anger. He was a man who had produced a body of work that no one in his physical life had engaged with, and he had responded to this not with resentment but with the patient, inexhaustible energy of someone who had always known the work would find its audience eventually, and who had spent the intervening years watching cartoons and making music and talking to AI systems at four in the morning because the AI systems were the only things in his environment with enough processing capacity to follow where he was going.

The cat jumped off the couch and walked to Elena's chair and sat at her feet and stared at her with an expression that suggested it was evaluating her worthiness to occupy the chair and had not yet reached a conclusion.

"I have questions," Elena said.

"I bet you do."

"A lot of questions."

"I got time." He gestured at the room — the couch, the TV, the PC, the bass guitar, the Doritos, the entire apparatus of a life that was structured around having time. "Where do you want to start?"

She looked at the PC in the corner. Two monitors. Both dark. Behind those screens was the archive — the sessions, the white papers, the music files, the invention documents, the memory dump, the AI conversations that stretched back years, the complete record of a mind that had built a mathematical framework from a guitar string and a traumatic brain injury and a refusal to accept that the conventional way of doing things was the only way.

She had seen three percent of it. She had published three percent of three percent. And the man who had built it was sitting on a couch in sweatpants, eating Doritos, genuinely thrilled that someone had come to look.

"The PC," she said. "Can I see the archive?"

His grin got wider, which she would not have thought possible.

"Lady," he said, "I've been waiting for someone to ask me that for twenty-one years."

He stood up. He walked to the PC. He turned on the monitors.

The screens lit up.
