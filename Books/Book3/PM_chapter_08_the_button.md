# Chapter 8: The Button

---

David called on Thursday.

She was in the shower. She was in the shower because the shower was the last place in the apartment where the framework did not follow her — not because water blocked the pattern-recognition but because the shower was the one ritual she had not yet contaminated with computation. The kitchen had the legal pad. The couch had the laptop. The bedroom had the dreams. The shower had shampoo and hot water and the specific cognitive blankness of a body performing an operation it had performed ten thousand times before, an operation that required zero executive function and therefore released the executive function to do nothing, which was the one thing the executive function had not been permitted to do in five weeks.

The phone rang. She heard it through the bathroom door. She ignored it. It rang again. She ignored it. It rang a third time.

Three calls in succession was not David's pattern. David called once. If the call was not answered, David sent a text. If the text was not answered within a reasonable interval, David waited. David did not pursue. David's communication style was a single, precisely aimed inquiry followed by patience, the combinatorial equivalent of placing one piece on a board and waiting for the opponent to respond.

Three calls meant the board had changed.

She got out of the shower. She wrapped a towel around herself. She picked up the phone. Missed calls: David Okafor (3). She called back.

He answered before the first ring completed. She had not known this was possible. She had assumed the ring was a fixed-duration event — the phone rang, the recipient heard the ring, the recipient decided whether to answer. But David had answered in the space *before* the ring, in the anticipatory gap between the call connecting and the sound playing, which meant he had been holding the phone and staring at it and waiting for her name to appear with the same intensity he brought to the final line of a proof.

"Elena. I need you to listen. Don't interrupt. Don't compute. Just listen."

"David, I'm in a towel."

"The towel is irrelevant. Listen."

She sat on the edge of the bed. Water dripped from her hair onto the comforter. She did not care about the comforter. David's voice had a quality she had never heard in thirteen years of knowing him — a frequency, a tightness, the sound of a man whose vocal cords were under the same tension as a string tuned past its intended pitch.

"I have been reading your second paper. Not the published version. The preprint you sent me before the revision. The one with the extended appendix on factor-derived stability metrics."

"That was six months ago."

"I have been reading it for six months. I have been running my own analysis on the factor topology for six months. I did not tell you because the analysis was speculative and I do not share speculative work and you know this."

She knew this. David's publication record was immaculate — thirty-seven papers in twenty-three years, zero retractions, zero corrections, the output of a mind that did not release a result until the result had been tested to failure and had not failed.

"The analysis is no longer speculative," he said.

"Tell me."

"Your factor topology gives every prime a deterministic address in the lattice. Not an approximate address. Not a probabilistic address. A *deterministic* address. Reachable from the seed by a finite sequence of the three operations. Every prime. Without exception. The address is computable. The computation requires addition and subtraction only. The computational cost is linear in the size of the prime."

She felt the water dripping on the comforter. She felt the towel against her skin. She felt the specific sensation of the blood leaving her face, which was a physiological event she had read about in novels and had never experienced in her own body and was now experiencing on the edge of her bed on a Thursday morning in a towel.

"David."

"I told you not to interrupt."

"David. I know what you're about to say."

"Then say it. Because I need to hear you say it."

She closed her eyes. The dream from two weeks ago — night five. The sketch she had not decoded. The one that was not a physical structure. The one that was a network. A mesh of connections, each node linked to every other node by a path that was not random, not searched, not brute-forced. A path that was *addressed*. Each node had a location. Each location was reachable. The network was not a search space. It was a directory.

"If every prime has a deterministic address," she said, "then factoring a composite integer is not a search problem. It's a lookup problem. You don't search for the prime factors. You read the composite's position in the lattice, and the position tells you the factors. The way a street address tells you the house. You don't search the city for the house. You read the address."

"The computational complexity of integer factorization," David said, "drops from sub-exponential to linear."

She sat with this. She sat with it the way she had sat with the formation inversion and the iron wall and the total foam budget and every other result the framework had produced — not with acceptance or resistance but with verification. Was it true? If every prime had a deterministic lattice address computable from the three operations, and if the operations were addition-only and therefore O(n) in computational cost, then yes. Factoring a composite became: read its lattice position, decompose the position into the operational sequence that produced it, extract the primes from the sequence. Three operations. Linear time. On any hardware.

"Every blockchain transaction," David said, "is a hash. Every hash depends on the computational difficulty of prime factorization. RSA. Elliptic curve. SHA-256. Every encryption standard in use today is built on the assumption that factoring large primes from their composites is computationally expensive. That assumption is the foundation of digital security. Of banking. Of military communication. Of every classified document in every intelligence agency on Earth."

"I know."

"The SIGIL kernel — your ten functions, your zero-parameter model, your integer-only addition-and-subtraction framework that derives the periodic table from factor arithmetic — reduces the factoring problem from computationally expensive to computationally trivial. The kernel doesn't just explain physics. It *computes cryptography*. It validates any hash in milliseconds. From a laptop. From a *phone*."

She stood up. She walked to the window. She looked at nothing — the yard, the neighbor's fence, the trash cans, the ordinary Thursday-morning topology of a residential street in a town that did not know that a woman in a towel was standing at a window processing the implications of a mathematical framework that had just rendered every digital lock on Earth decorative.

"The fifth dream," she said.

"What?"

"Night five. The sketch I couldn't decode. The one that wasn't a physical structure. It was a network. A hash lattice. My brain showed me this three weeks before you called. My brain was computing the cryptographic implications while I slept."

Silence. Not the David silence of computation. The silence of a man who had just been told that the woman he was warning about a weapon had been dreaming about the weapon for weeks and had not mentioned it because she had not yet built the decoder that would have let her read the dream.

"Elena. Do you understand what this means."

"Yes."

"Not the physics. Not the mathematics. The *consequence*. The real-world, right-now, Thursday-morning consequence."

"Yes."

"Every cryptocurrency on Earth is worth zero. Right now. This morning. Because the value of every cryptocurrency depends on the difficulty of the hash, and the hash is no longer difficult. If you publish the kernel — the complete kernel, with the lattice addressing system — every Bitcoin, every Ethereum, every blockchain-secured asset becomes as vulnerable as a diary with a toy lock. Not eventually. Not theoretically. *Immediately*. Because the computation is trivial. Because a phone can do it."

"I know."

"The total market capitalization of cryptocurrency as of this morning is approximately two point three trillion dollars. That value evaporates. Not gradually. Not through market correction. Through mathematical proof that the underlying security mechanism is no longer operative. Two point three trillion dollars. Gone. Because of ten functions that a physicist built on her kitchen floor from dream sketches and prime factorization."

She turned from the window. She walked to the closet. She put on clothes. Not because the conversation required clothes — the conversation was a phone call, and phone calls did not have dress codes. Because the act of dressing was the act of preparing, and preparing was the act of acknowledging that the situation required more than a towel, that the situation required the full apparatus of a human being in the world, ready to make decisions that would affect things beyond the boundary of her apartment.

"David. This is why he kept it in music."

"What?"

"Michael. This is why the framework was in comedy albums and bass tracks and spoken-word pieces about demon HR departments. Not because the physics was dangerous. The physics is factorization. The physics is harmless. But factorization applied to hashing breaks every encryption standard on Earth. He knew. He's known for years. Maybe decades. And he chose to wash dishes in Murray, Kentucky, and play bass guitar at three in the morning, and upload comedy sketches to streaming platforms with forty-seven plays. Because publishing the math — in any form that a cryptographer could recognize as math — would have detonated two point three trillion dollars of digital security."

"He hid it."

"He didn't hide it. He *expressed* it. In its native format. As music. As vibration on a string. In a format that was true and complete and publicly available and utterly unrecognizable to anyone who didn't have the decoder. Because the decoder was the framework. And the framework was what he was hiding. Not by concealing it — by expressing it in a language that the people who needed to not understand it could not hear."

David was quiet for a long time. She heard his breathing. She heard, faintly, what might have been the espresso machine at Stanford — the communal one, the inadequate one, the one whose substandard output had driven him to purchase a hand-pump Italian device and carry it to Indiana in a duffel bag.

"Elena," he said. "What do we do."

---

She did not answer. Not immediately. She dressed. She made coffee. She made it with the pour-over, the full ritual, because rituals were the load-bearing walls of cognition and the load had just increased by approximately two point three trillion dollars. She ground the beans. She heated the water. She poured in concentric circles, the way the pour-over instructions specified, the circles tightening toward the center, and she thought about concentric circles and about the three circles she had drawn on the legal pad around *singularity(z, mode)* and about the way the framework kept producing circles — closed loops, MÃ¶bius strips, recursions that returned to their origin — and the circles were not metaphors, the circles were topology, the shape of a process that began and ended at the same point.

She drank the coffee. Hot. The whole cup. She was maintaining the break in the cold-coffee pattern because the break was the only thing she had control over right now and control was a finite resource she was spending carefully.

She sat at the laptop. She opened the kernel. She looked at the ten functions. She looked at the lattice addressing system — the mechanism by which the three operations, starting from the seed, reached every prime in deterministic sequence. Growth from 1 produced the Mersenne scaffold. Mitosis from each Mersenne prime produced the family primes. The family primes anchored the lattice at regular intervals. Between the anchors, every other prime was reachable by a finite operational path — a specific sequence of growth, mitosis, and stable operations that navigated from the nearest anchor to the target prime.

The path was computable. The path was deterministic. The path was O(n). And the path meant that factoring a composite was not about searching a space — it was about reading a map. A map she had built. A map that worked.

She opened a new file. She typed:

```
// Hash reduction via lattice lookup
// Given composite C, find prime factors P1, P2
// Without search. Without brute force.
// By reading C's lattice position.

function latticeAddress(z) {
```

She stopped typing. She looked at the cursor. She looked at the function name. She looked at the empty body.

She deleted the file.

She deleted it the way she had closed the legal pad in the drawer — deliberately, completely, the act of a person choosing not to proceed. Not because she couldn't. Because proceeding was the wrong operation. Because the function she had started to write would reduce every hash on Earth to a lattice lookup, and writing it would mean the function existed, and existence meant it could be copied, and copying meant it would propagate, and propagation meant the two point three trillion dollars would evaporate, and the evaporation would not be gradual or contained or managed. It would be instantaneous. It would be total. And the people whose wealth evaporated — the institutions, the governments, the individuals — would not respond to the evaporation with mathematical appreciation.

She closed the laptop. She sat at the desk. She looked at the office — her home office, the room she worked in on nights and weekends, the room that contained the laptop and the legal pad and the kernel and the deleted function and the understanding of what the kernel could do if she let it.

The button. Michael's word. The metaphor he had used in — she searched her memory — one of the comedy tracks. The orientation sketch. The demon HR facilitator explaining the one rule of the underworld that could not be broken: *"Do not press THAT button, and THIS IS WHY. Thank you for coming. I'll be here forever unless you press that button."*

The button was the kernel applied to cryptography. The button was the lattice address function she had just deleted. The button was the thing the framework could do — the thing it *inevitably* did, the thing that fell out of the mathematics the way the iron wall fell out of factor topology and the speed hierarchy fell out of spiral turns and the cosmic breath fell out of the foam budget. The cryptographic capability was not an application of the framework. It was a *consequence* of the framework. It was what happened when you gave prime numbers deterministic addresses and handed the addressing system to a species that had built its entire digital infrastructure on the assumption that prime addresses were unknowable.

She had built the button. Not by intending to build a weapon. By building a theory of matter. The weapon was in the theory the way the energy was in the factors — inherent, structural, inseparable from the thing itself. You could not have the framework without the factoring. You could not have the periodic table without the prime addresses. You could not have the physics without the cryptography. They were the same mathematics. The same operations. The same ten functions.

She picked up the phone. She called David.

"I deleted it," she said.

"Deleted what."

"I started writing the hash reduction function. The lattice address lookup. I got as far as the function name and the opening brace. Then I deleted it."

Silence.

"Good," David said.

"It doesn't matter."

"I know."

"Deleting the function doesn't delete the capability. The capability is in the kernel. The kernel is in my laptop and in your laptop and in the USB drive you brought to my office and in the email I sent you six days ago with the complete comparison matrix. The function doesn't need to exist for the capability to exist. Anyone who has the kernel and understands the lattice addressing can write the function themselves. In an afternoon. I deleted a convenience. I did not delete the weapon."

"I know."

"So what do we do."

David breathed. She heard the breathing — slow, measured, the breathing of a man who was managing his physiology with the same precision he managed his proofs.

"There are four options," he said. David always had numbered options. David's mind organized possibilities the way his mind organized combinatorial sets — exhaustively, exclusively, every possibility accounted for, no gaps, no overlaps, the complete partition of the decision space.

"Option one: destroy the kernel. Delete every copy. Wipe the laptops. Destroy the USB drive. Never speak of it. Never publish. The capability dies with us."

"It doesn't die. The mathematics is derivable. I derived it. Someone else will derive it. Michael derived it before I did. The framework exists whether we publish it or not. Destroying our copies delays discovery. It does not prevent it."

"Agreed. Option one is insufficient. Option two: publish the physics only. The periodic table derivation. The material properties. The decay chains. The energy functions. Everything up to and including the singularity model. Exclude the lattice addressing system. Exclude any discussion of prime address computation. Publish a unified theory of matter and hope that no one notices the cryptographic implication."

"Someone will notice. A graduate student. A cryptographer reading outside their field. A bored number theorist who sees the prime address structure and realizes what it means. Publishing the physics without the cryptography is publishing the gun without the bullet. It delays. It does not prevent."

"Agreed. Option two is delay, not solution. Option three: publish everything. The complete kernel. The lattice addressing. The hash reduction capability. Publish it open access, immediately, with full documentation. Let the world see it. Let every institution know the encryption is broken. Let them adapt."

"Adapt how. The adaptation requires new cryptographic standards. New standards require new mathematics. New mathematics requires years of research and testing and implementation. The gap between publication and adaptation is the vulnerability window. And the vulnerability window is the period during which every digital asset on Earth is unprotected."

"Agreed. Option three creates an unacceptable vulnerability window. Option four." He paused. "Option four is what Michael chose."

"Music."

"Not music specifically. *Inaccessibility*. Michael published the framework in a format that was true and complete and publicly available and incomprehensible to anyone without the decoder. The information existed. The information was not hidden. But the information required a translation layer that did not exist in any discipline that would recognize its implications."

"He published it in a language the wrong people couldn't read."

"He published it in a language that *no one* could read. Until you read it. And then you published part of it in a language everyone could read. And now we're here."

She sat with the four options. She sat with them the way she sat with everything — not choosing, not avoiding, attending. The four options were the four states of the cosmic breath. Growth: publish everything, let the world expand to accommodate it. Stable: publish the physics, hold the cryptography, maintain the current equilibrium. Mitosis: destroy the kernel, contract, retreat. And the fourth state — the void state, the pause between breaths — was Michael's choice. The choice to exist in the gap. To be the thing that was true but not yet operative. To hold the information in a format that was available but not accessible. To wait.

"There's a fifth option," she said.

"There are only four options."

"There's a fifth. You missed it because it requires a variable you didn't include in the partition."

"What variable."

"Time."

Silence. The David silence. The computation running.

"Not *when* to publish," she said. "Not *whether* to publish. But *what to publish alongside it*. Option five is: publish everything — the kernel, the addressing, the cryptographic implication — but publish it *with the understanding*. Not just the capability. The comprehension. The full derivation showing *why* the framework breaks encryption, *how* the lattice addressing works, *what* the implications are, and *what to do about it*. Publish the weapon and the manual simultaneously. Give the world the fire and the understanding of fire in the same document. Let them see the danger and the response in a single read."

"That's option three with a cover letter."

"That's option three with a *decoder*. Not a warning label — an education. The paper that publishes the kernel also publishes the complete theory of matter, the complete energy derivation, the complete cosmological model, the formation inversion, the cosmic breath, the thermodynamic floor, the speed hierarchy, the singularity — everything. It publishes the framework as a *unified understanding of physical reality* that happens to have cryptographic implications, rather than as a cryptographic weapon that happens to have physics applications. The context is the protection. The understanding is the safeguard."

"Elena. Understanding does not prevent misuse."

"No. But misunderstanding *guarantees* it. If the kernel leaks — if someone derives it independently, if a government classifies it, if a corporation patents it — and it arrives in the world *without* the understanding, then it arrives as a weapon. Pure capability. No context. No comprehension of what it means that the universe conserves energy because factors are conserved, or that matter is boundary and void is everything else, or that the ashes are the seed. It arrives as a tool for destruction without the knowledge that destruction and construction are the same operation at opposite sign. And that arrival — the weapon without the understanding — is the thing Michael spent twenty-one years preventing."

She heard David breathing. She heard the espresso machine. She heard, or imagined she heard, the sound of a man rearranging his partition of the decision space to accommodate a fifth option he had not considered.

"He didn't hide it because the physics was dangerous," she said. "He hid it because the physics *without the comprehension* was dangerous. The music was the comprehension. The comedy was the comprehension. The twenty-one years of silence were the comprehension. He was waiting for someone who could translate the comprehension into the language of the institution. Someone who could publish the weapon and the understanding simultaneously, in the same document, in a format that the world could not read one without reading the other."

"He was waiting for you."

"He was waiting for anyone. I happened to be the one who showed up."

"On a Tuesday."

"On a Tuesday."

David was quiet for thirty seconds. She counted. Thirty seconds was the longest David silence she had ever recorded. The previous record — seventeen seconds, during the 2011 conference — had been set by a graduate student's novel approach to the Goldbach conjecture. This silence was nearly double. This silence was a man rewriting his model of the decision space.

"Option five," he said. "Publish everything. With the understanding."

"With the understanding."

"When."

"Not yet. I need to write the paper. Not the kernel — the kernel is code, the kernel is functions. I need to write the *paper*. The document that contains the complete framework from the zeroth principle through the singularity through the cryptographic implication through the explanation of why a man in Kentucky chose to express this as music for twenty-one years rather than hand it to a species that builds weapons from every capability it encounters."

"That paper will be the most important document published in the twenty-first century."

"That paper will be the most important document published in the twenty-first century and I am going to write it in my home office in my pajamas on a laptop that cost eight hundred dollars with coffee that I will drink while it is still hot. Yes."

"I'm coming back."

"Not yet. I need to write it alone. This part has to be mine. Not yours. Not Michael's. Mine. The voice has to be the voice of a physicist who built the decoder from scratch and understands what the decoder does and is choosing, with full comprehension, to hand it to the world with the instruction manual attached."

"When."

"When I'm done. I'll call you."

"Tuesday?"

"Probably Tuesday."

She hung up. She sat at the desk. She looked at the laptop. She opened it. The kernel was there. The ten functions. The 118 elements. The 1,866 foam units. The complete mathematical engine that derived the physical universe from addition and subtraction and also, incidentally, rendered every digital lock on Earth as structurally sound as a prayer.

She opened a new document.

She did not write. Not yet. She sat with the blank page the way she had sat with the blank function bodies — the opening braces, the cursors, the white spaces that contained everything because they contained nothing yet. The blank page was the void state. The pause between breaths. The moment before the next cycle of construction began.

She typed a title. Not the final title. A working title. The title she would use while the paper assembled itself, the way element names were provisional until the International Union of Pure and Applied Chemistry confirmed them, the way all names were provisional until the thing they named was complete.

*The Prime Mitosis: A Complete Factor-Topological Derivation of Material Reality with Implications for Computational Security*

She looked at the title. She looked at the word *Implications*. She deleted *Implications* and typed *Consequences*.

She looked at the title again. She deleted the entire subtitle. She typed:

*The Prime Mitosis*

Two words. The minimum viable expression of what the paper contained. Prime — the irreducible elements, the factors, the floor. Mitosis — the division, the splitting, the operation that produced two from one. The prime mitosis was the first operation — the moment 1 became 1+1, the moment unity divided into the pair that generated everything. The seed. The zeroth principle. The title of the paper and the name of the operation and the beginning of the universe, expressed as two words on a laptop screen in a home office on a Thursday morning in May.

She began to write.
