Einstein called it "spukhafte Fernwirkung" — spooky action at a distance. He meant it as a criticism. Quantum entanglement, the phenomenon where measuring one particle instantaneously determines the state of another particle regardless of the distance between them, seemed to violate everything Einstein believed about locality and the speed of light. If information cannot travel faster than c, how can a measurement HERE instantaneously affect a particle THERE?

The physics community spent the next ninety years arguing about it. Bell's theorem, the Aspect experiment, loophole-free tests, interpretations stacked on interpretations. The phenomenon is real — experimentally verified beyond any serious dispute. The mechanism remains officially mysterious.

## THE ACOUSTIC SINGULARITY

Both fingers feel the vibration simultaneously. Not "almost simultaneously." Not "at the speed of sound through steel." SIMULTANEOUSLY. The string is one body under tension. A perturbation at any point on the string equalizes across the entire string as a UNIT. The string does not transmit the vibration from one end to the other as a traveling wave that propagates at finite speed. The string responds as a WHOLE BODY — every point adjusting together because the tension binds every point to every other point and the adjustment is structural, not propagational.

This is an acoustic singularity: a wholistic body under tension that transmits perturbation simultaneously across its entire extent. The key requirements are: the body must be continuous (no breaks in the string), under tension (taut, no slack), and elastic (the perturbation equalizes rather than breaking the structure). If these three conditions hold, the body is an acoustic singularity — a single structure that responds as one thing regardless of its spatial extent.

The guitar string between the nut and the bridge is an acoustic singularity. The nut and the bridge are its two boundary points. A perturbation at either boundary is felt at both boundaries simultaneously, because the string between them is one continuous, taut, elastic body.
