# Chapter 2: Murray

---

The drive from Nashville to Murray was three hours if you respected the speed limit, which Elena did, because she was constitutionally incapable of not respecting limits, even ones she suspected were arbitrary.

She had landed at BNA at 11:40 AM on a Thursday. The rental car was a silver Nissan Sentra that smelled like synthetic pine and someone else's breakfast sandwich. She adjusted the mirrors, connected her phone, and pulled up directions to Murray, Kentucky, population approximately 19,000, home of Murray State University and the Calloway County Courthouse where she had, eight months ago, confirmed the death of a man who appeared to be sending her encrypted mathematical corrections from the afterlife.

She had not called ahead. She did not know who she would call. The county records listed a Michael Shaffer, deceased, with no surviving spouse and no emergency contact. The cemetery records listed a plot purchased and a stone erected and a body interred, and the cemetery administrator had been helpful and sympathetic and entirely certain. Elena had stood at the grave. She had read the inscription. She had photographed it.

The inscription was still in her phone. She did not need to look at it.

*If you are reading this, you are as crazy as I am... and it is already too late. Enjoy the gift.*

She merged onto I-24 West and set the cruise control to seventy-two.

---

The problem with driving three hours alone is that the mind rehearses.

Elena rehearsed conversations. She had been rehearsing them since the airport — since the flight, since the night before the flight, since the moment she had read the margin note (*Peer? The FUCK you talkin bout WILLIS?*) and understood that whatever she was driving toward was not a resolution but an escalation.

She rehearsed the widow scenario. There would be a woman, late fifties or early sixties, living in the house the county records associated with the Shaffer name. She would be cautious at the door. Elena would introduce herself — *I'm a physicist, I published a paper based on your husband's work, I'm sorry for your loss, I have some questions about his mathematical archive.* The widow would invite her in or she wouldn't. There would be tea. There would be photographs on the mantel. There would be the careful, guarded hospitality of a Southern town that did not discuss its own with strangers — she had experienced this hospitality eight months ago and it had been impenetrable. But she had not had the correction then. She had not had four pages of handwritten proof in notation that matched a dead man's voice. That was a different kind of credential.

She rehearsed the brother scenario. An older man, similar features, same handwriting — genetics could account for the notation style if you squinted hard enough and wanted to. A brother who had inherited the archive. Who had read Elena's paper and found the weak point and sent the fix, anonymously, because the family had its reasons for anonymity and the reasons were none of her business. This was the scenario she preferred because it was the most normal and the least interesting and would allow her to return to her office and her grant applications and her life without having to reconsider anything fundamental about the nature of death.

She rehearsed the student scenario. A protégé. Someone Michael had taught before he died — if he had died — someone who carried the notation and the voice and the combative margin-scrawling because they had learned it from the source. A mathematical child. This scenario had the advantage of explaining the correction and the disadvantage of raising the question of why a student capable of extending the kernel convergence proof to the infinite case had not published independently, had not surfaced in the literature, had not appeared at any conference, had not done any of the things a person with that level of capability would normally do.

She rehearsed the impossible scenario. She rehearsed it briefly and clinically, the way she would describe an experimental result that contradicted the model: *the data are inconsistent with the null hypothesis. Further investigation required.*

The null hypothesis was that Michael Shaffer was dead.

The data were inconsistent.

She did not rehearse what she would say to a living man. She did not know what you said to a person whose work you had published, whose death you had confirmed, whose grave you had visited, whose framework had reorganized your understanding of number theory and topology and possibly theology and whose margin notes included the word FUCK in capital letters and a reference to a person or concept called Willis that she still had not identified.

*Hello, I published your life's work under my name and stood at your grave and now you're alive and I have questions* did not seem adequate.

She passed a billboard for the Creation Museum. She did not stop.

---

Kentucky was beautiful in a way that surprised her every time. She had grown up in cities — Phoenix, then Boston, then the various institutional geometries of MIT and Chicago and the campus where she now held tenure. Her landscape vocabulary was concrete and glass and the particular shade of grey that universities painted everything. Kentucky was green. Aggressively, unapologetically, almost confrontationally green, as if the state had decided to make a philosophical argument about the relationship between neglect and beauty and was winning.

The road narrowed. The GPS directed her through towns that were not quite towns — a gas station, a church, a Dollar General, a field, another church, another field. She passed a hand-painted sign that read JESUS SAVES and another that read FIREWOOD $40/CORD and wondered if the proximity was intentional.

She was stalling. She knew she was stalling. She had been driving for two hours and forty minutes and the GPS said seventeen minutes remaining and her hands were steady on the wheel and her breathing was even and her mind was running the same rehearsal loops it had been running since Nashville, and she was stalling because the moment she arrived she would have to knock on a door and discover which scenario was real and the discovery would be irreversible and she was a physicist who dealt in irreversible measurements and this should not have been a problem.

It was a problem.

She thought about calling David. David would be calm. David would say something grounding and precise and she would feel better for approximately ninety seconds and then she would feel worse because she would have to explain why she was in Kentucky again, alone, chasing the ghost of an author she had confirmed dead, and David would not say *you're being irrational* because David never said things like that, but he would think it, and she would hear him thinking it, and the hearing would be worse than the saying.

She did not call David.

She turned onto 641 South. The road straightened. The fields opened up on both sides, flat and wide and scattered with the kind of fencing that existed less to contain anything and more to establish the principle of containment. The sky was enormous. She had forgotten how enormous the sky was in places where buildings did not interrupt it.

Murray appeared the way small towns appear — not all at once but by accumulation. A Walmart. A Lowe's. A Kroger. Then the university campus, redbrick and deliberate, and then the town itself, which was smaller than she remembered and warmer than she expected and exactly as indifferent to her arrival as it had been eight months ago.

She checked into the same motel on 12th Street. The woman at the desk did not recognize her. Elena paid with her university card, carried her bag to the room, set it on the bed, and stood at the window looking at the parking lot.

The parking lot offered no insight.

---

She had brought the correction. The four pages were in a manila folder in her carry-on, protected between hardcovers, and she removed them now and spread them on the motel desk. The handwriting was cramped and certain. The notation was nonstandard. The proof was complete.

She read it again. She had read it eleven times. Each time, the proof held and the voice clarified. The voice was not theoretical. It was not constructed from pattern-matching or wishful inference. It was the same voice she had inhabited for three months — the transcript voice, the archive voice, the voice that insisted on X+X+1 instead of 2X+1 because the form of the operation carried information that the algebraic reduction destroyed.

There was a moment, somewhere around the sixth reading, when she had stopped asking *is this his voice?* and started asking *why is his voice not dead?*

She looked at the desk. She looked at the motel room — same layout, same polyester bedspread, same window unit air conditioner that rattled when it cycled. She had sat in this room eight months ago and read the memory dump for the first time. She had sat here and understood that the framework was not a theory but a mind. She had sat here and opened her laptop and begun writing the preliminary evaluation that would become the forty-second paper that would change everything that would bring her back to this same desk in this same room with four handwritten pages and no explanation.

She was going in circles. Recursive. The structure was recursive.

*Stop it,* she told herself. *Not everything is a metaphor for the framework.*

She showered. She changed clothes. She ate a granola bar from her carry-on. She looked at the address she had pulled from the county records — the last known residence associated with Michael Shaffer, a rental on the south side of town. She put the address in her phone. She put the four pages back in the manila folder. She put the folder in her bag.

She did not rehearse. She was finished rehearsing.

She drove to the address. It was a ten-minute drive through the kind of residential streets that existed in every small town in America — houses that were not quite old and not quite new, yards that were not quite kept and not quite abandoned, the particular equilibrium of a place that had decided what it was and was not interested in revising the decision.

She parked across the street. The house was single-story, beige siding, a porch with a single chair and a coffee can that might have been an ashtray. The windows were lit. A television was on — she could see the blue flicker through the curtains. Someone was home.

Elena sat in the rental car with the engine off and her hands on the steering wheel and looked at the lit windows of a house in Murray, Kentucky, and thought about the inscription on the tombstone and the voice in the margin notes and the proof that completed the proof that completed the framework that completed the structure that had no completion because it was recursive and the recursive had no end, only depth.

She got out of the car. She crossed the street. She walked up the three concrete steps to the porch. She stood at the door.

She knocked.
